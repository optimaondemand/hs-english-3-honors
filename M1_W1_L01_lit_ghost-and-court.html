📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A prince stands in a bright court, dressed in black, and tells everyone they do not know what he is.
This year asks one question from many angles: what does it mean to govern yourself? We begin with a man who cannot stop examining his own mind. In the first two scenes of Hamlet, a ghost walks the castle walls and a new king smiles over a fresh grave. Your work today is to read those scenes and to start tracking the gap this whole play runs on: the distance between how things seem and how they are.

📚 PART 1 · A WARM-UP BEFORE WE BEGIN
💭 QUICK CHECK-IN (no wrong answer)
Think of a time you had to keep your real feelings hidden in public. Which was closer to the truth: what you showed, or what you held inside?

What a soliloquy does

On a stage, a character can lie to every other character and still tell the audience the truth. That is the work of the soliloquy: a speech delivered while alone, thinking aloud, with no one to perform for. It is the one place in a play where public words fall away and we hear the private mind. Hamlet is built on this device. In public, its hero is watched by a king who took the throne under a shadow, so Hamlet guards his face; alone, he lets us see what the court cannot. As you read, keep two columns in mind: what Hamlet shows others, and what he shows only us.

🏛️ SITUATE THE TEXT: WHO, WHEN, AND WHERE
The Chandos portrait, attributed to John Taylor, c. 1611. Public domain.
William Shakespeare (1564–1616)

Shakespeare was born in Stratford-upon-Avon, England, and built his career in London as an actor, a part-owner of a playing company, and the most celebrated playwright of his age. He wrote Hamlet around 1600 for an open-air London playhouse, the Globe, which had almost no scenery. There were no spotlights and no film close-ups, so the language did the work a camera does now. Hamlet also belongs to a popular form of the day, the revenge tragedy, in which a wronged figure is called, often by a ghost, to avenge a murder and is usually destroyed in the attempt. Audiences knew the pattern. Part of what makes Hamlet strange to its first viewers is how long its hero refuses to follow it.

When is the play set? Shakespeare never names a year. He built Hamlet from an old Scandinavian legend of a Danish prince, a story older than the year 1200, so its world feels late-medieval. But he stitched in details from his own Renaissance: Hamlet and his friend Horatio study at the University of Wittenberg, a real school that existed in Shakespeare's own lifetime, not the distant past. Read the setting as a Denmark out of legend, seen through Renaissance eyes.
🗺️ THE MAP OF HAMLET'S WORLD

The action never leaves the Danish court, but its threats and escapes come from the countries around it. Denmark is a peninsula (Jutland) plus islands; the castle of Elsinore (Kronborg) stands at the northeast tip of the island of Zealand, on the narrow strait facing Sweden.

Abraham Ortelius, Daniae Regni Typus (a map of the kingdom of Denmark), first printed about 1570 in the Theatrum Orbis Terrarum, the first modern atlas. This is a Renaissance-era map, made within a generation of when Shakespeare wrote Hamlet, and it is provided here so you can see Denmark as mapmakers of the play's own age pictured it. 👀 PART 2 · THE ENGINE OF THE PLAY: SEEMING VS. BEING

Early in scene 2, the new king and queen tell Hamlet to stop grieving so visibly. Look at how he answers. His public words are careful and cold; the truth he keeps for himself is enormous. This single exchange sets up everything to come.

🕶️ WHAT THE COURT IS MEANT TO SEE
A young prince in mourning black, told his grief is now excessive and that he should rejoin the family.
His guarded, double-edged replies: "A little more than kin, and less than kind," and "I am too much i' the sun."
💭 WHAT HAMLET HOLDS WITHIN
A grief and suspicion so deep that the outward signs cannot begin to show it.
His insistence: "Seems, madam? Nay, it is; I know not 'seems,'" and "I have that within which passeth show."
Appearance vs. reality is the machine the whole play runs on. In every scene, ask: who is performing, and for whom?

Now read scenes 1 and 2

Read Act I, scenes 1 and 2. In scene 1, watch the cold, fearful watch on the battlements and the silent Ghost that will not answer; notice how Horatio explains the war-footing with Norway and young Fortinbras. In scene 2, hear Claudius balance a funeral and a wedding in the same smooth breath, then watch Hamlet refuse to play along. Mark the word seems each time it appears.

✍️ PART 3 · DO SOMETHING WITH THE READING

Hamlet's very first words are a muttered aside about his uncle, the new king, who has married his mother: "A little more than kin, and less than kind." Two small words, kin and kind, carry the whole mood.

📓
READING JOURNAL
In your Journal, write two or three sentences on this line. To do it well: say what kin means (family), then weigh the two senses of kind (affectionate, and "of the same nature"). Then explain what the pun lets Hamlet do in front of the king he distrusts. Quote the words you are reading; a claim without the words is only a guess.
🤖 WORKING WITH AI

An AI tool can summarize these scenes in seconds and define a word like kind. What it cannot do is read the line in your own voice or notice the pun for you. It also invents things: ask it for a quotation and it may hand you a line that is not in the play. Use it, if at all, to check a definition; then confirm every claim against the actual text before you write it down.
