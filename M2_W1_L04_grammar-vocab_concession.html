📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
The strongest arguments admit what the other side gets right, then answer it. A few connecting words make that move for you.
This quarter you write arguments, so this week's grammar is the grammar of argument: subordinating conjunctions and transitions that concede and counter. Words like although, while, and however let you grant a point to the other side and then turn to your own, in one controlled sentence. You will use this move immediately in Feeder 3.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to use a subordinating conjunction to concede a point in a single sentence.
2
I will be able to punctuate a concession-and-counter sentence correctly.
3
I will be able to use two words from No. 10 precisely in my own sentences.
✏️ PART 1 · THE CONCESSION-AND-COUNTER SENTENCE

To concede is to grant that the other side has a fair point; to counter is to answer it. Doing both in one sentence makes you sound fair and in command. The tool is a subordinating conjunction (although, though, while, even if, whereas): it attaches the opposing point as a subordinate clause, leaving your own claim in the main clause, where the emphasis lands. When the concession comes first, put a comma after it.

THE FRAME
Although factions can never be removed, [comma]  their effects can be controlled by good design.
Blue = the concession (the other side's point), held in a subordinate clause. Gold = your claim, in the main clause where the stress falls. The subordinating word does the work; the comma marks the seam.

You can also counter across two sentences with a transition: "Madison grants that liberty feeds faction. Even so, he refuses to blame liberty." Either way, the pattern is the same: name the fair point, then turn.

📕 PART 2 · WORD STUDY (from No. 10)
WordIn the textWhat it meansWord origin
latent
adjective		 Madison writes that "the latent causes of faction are thus sown in the nature of man" (Federalist 10). Present but hidden or not yet active; lying beneath the surface, waiting to appear. From Latin latentem, from latere ("to lie hidden"); the same root survives in "latency," meaning present but not yet visible or active.
propensity
noun		 He describes the human "propensity" to fall into division and mutual animosity (Federalist 10). A natural tendency or inclination to behave in a certain way. From Latin propensus ("inclined, hanging forward"), from pro- ("forward") and pendere ("to hang"); literally "hanging toward" something, hence a leaning or tendency.
📓
READING JOURNAL · WORD STUDY
Add both words to your Journal. For each, write one new sentence of your own on a subject unrelated to the Federalist, keeping the meaning exact and making the sentence show the meaning. For latent, aim for the sense of something hidden but ready to surface; for propensity, a genuine tendency, not a one-time act. Notice how latent's root sense of "lying hidden" still echoes in anything described as latent today.
⚖️ PART 3 · NOT EVERY ARGUMENT REASONS LIKE MADISON

Madison's argument holds up because each step follows from real evidence. Not every argument about factions and republics is built so carefully. Three common shortcuts in reasoning: a post hoc argument assumes that because one event followed another, the first caused it. A circular argument uses its conclusion as its own proof. A red herring answers a claim by changing the subject entirely.

📧 PART 4 · THE COUSIN GENRE: FORMAL CORRESPONDENCE

Later this module you will write Publius Returns, a public op-ed. A formal letter is a related but distinct form: same aim (persuade or request something in writing), a different register and shape. Real citizens, including ones arguing exactly the way Madison does, use both. Every formal letter follows the same skeleton:

THE SKELETON OF A FORMAL LETTER
Heading — your address and the date
Inside address — the recipient's name, title, and address
Salutation — "Dear [Name/Title]," followed by a colon
Body — state your purpose, make your case (a concession-and-counter sentence fits perfectly here), and say exactly what you want done
Closing — "Sincerely," or "Respectfully," then your signature
A short model, using today's concession-and-counter move in the body:
Dear Editor,

Although I understand the district's budget is limited, cutting the school's only civics elective sends the wrong signal about what we value. I ask the board to reconsider before the fall schedule is finalized.

Sincerely,
A Concerned Student

Notice what changes between a letter and an op-ed: a letter names one specific reader and asks for one specific action; an op-ed addresses the general public and argues a public position. Same persuasive tools, different address and shape.

📓
READING JOURNAL
Write a short formal letter of your own (four to six sentences in the body), using the full skeleton above: heading, inside address, salutation, body, closing. Address it to a real or invented recipient (a school board member, a company, a newspaper editor) about something you actually have an opinion on, and build one concession-and-counter sentence into the body, the same move you practiced earlier in this lesson.
