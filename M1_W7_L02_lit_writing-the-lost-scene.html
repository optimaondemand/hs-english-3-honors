📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Every play has doors it never opens: a character offstage, a silence, a scene we only hear about. Your project walks through one of those doors.
For your Module 1 project, the Elsinore Stage, you join Shakespeare's company and write an original missing scene or soliloquy for a secondary character: Ophelia, Laertes, Gertrude, Horatio, or Fortinbras. This is not fan fiction; it is a form of interpretation. To write a character's hidden moment convincingly, you have to understand them as well as Shakespeare did. Today you learn how the project works and how to find the right gap to fill.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to identify a genuine gap in the play where a lost scene could plausibly live.
2
I will be able to list what my scene must stay consistent with in the real play.
3
I will be able to plan a scene in blank verse that carries one clear point of view.
📚 PART 1 · WHAT THE PROJECT IS

Over Weeks 8 and 9 you will draft and then finalize a short original piece, roughly 20 to 40 lines, that could slot into the real Hamlet: a soliloquy Ophelia never gets, the letter-scene Horatio only reports, Fortinbras's council before he marches. You will write it in loose blank verse (the five-beat line from Week 1), work in at least one named rhetorical figure such as antithesis, and hold a single clear point of view so the character wants something on the page. This is imitation in the oldest sense: you learn an author's craft by writing in their manner. The goal is not parody but fidelity, catching how your character actually thinks and speaks.

🧩 PART 2 · ANATOMY OF A LOST SCENE

A convincing lost scene answers three questions before you write a line. Use your Week 4 Character Dossier to answer them.

THREE QUESTIONS EVERY LOST SCENE MUST ANSWER
1. Where does it slot in?
Name the exact seam in the real play: after which scene, during which offstage event. The gap should be real, a place the play genuinely leaves open, not a contradiction of what we see.
2. Whose voice, and what do they want here?
Your character must want something in this moment, and it should grow out of the want you traced in your dossier. Give them a reason to speak now.
3. What must it not contradict?
List the facts your scene has to respect: what the character knows and does not know at that point, and what the real play shows happening before and after. Consistency is what makes it feel like Shakespeare's, not yours pasted in.
🎭 PART 3 · HEAR THE REGISTER

A student drafting a lost scene for Horatio, mourning quietly after Hamlet's death, tried four different opening lines. Only one holds the play's register: loose blank verse, elevated but plain diction, feeling shown rather than reported. Read all four, then choose the one that actually sounds like it could stand in the play.

💬 WHICH DRAFT LINE BEST IMITATES THE PLAY'S VOICE?
Four student drafts for the same moment. Which one belongs in Elsinore?
📓
READING JOURNAL
Plan your lost scene by answering the three questions in your Journal. Name the exact seam where your scene slots in; name your character and the one thing they want in that moment; and list two or three facts from the real play your scene must not contradict. Pull from your Week 4 Character Dossier. This plan becomes your Week 8 draft, so make it specific.
🤖 WORKING WITH CLAUDE ON THIS PROJECT

The writing of this scene must be your own; that is the whole assignment. The Elsinore Stage Workshop page has a built-in step for using Claude the right way: it can check your lines for rhythm or plot contradictions, never generate or rewrite them. You will do that step, and log it, when you get there.
