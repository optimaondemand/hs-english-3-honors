Lesson 7.03 · Elocution Craft
Speaking the Verse
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Memorizing the words is half the work. The other half is deciding what they should sound like.
A recitation is an interpretation you perform. Reading the verse aloud well means letting the meaning, not the metronome, lead: pausing where the sense pauses, leaning on the words that matter, and trusting silence. Today you learn to mark a passage for delivery and rehearse it, so your graded recitation carries the thought, not just the lines.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to pause at the sense, not automatically at the end of each verse line.
2
I will be able to choose which words to stress so a line means what I intend.
3
I will be able to mark my own passage for pace, pause, and emphasis.
🎤 PART 1 · LET THE SENSE LEAD

The most common recitation mistake is stopping at the end of every line because it looks like where to breathe. Verse does not work that way. When a sentence runs past the line break (this is called enjambment), keep going and pause where the punctuation and the meaning tell you to. Three habits carry a performance: pause at the sense, not the line-end; stress the two or three words a line truly turns on; and vary your pace, slowing for weight and letting lighter runs move. Below, one line is marked the way an actor might mark a script.

MARKING A LINE FOR DELIVERY
To be, ( / ) or not to be, ( // ) that is the question
Gold = words to stress.   ( / ) = short pause, ( // ) = longer pause.
The pauses fall at the commas, not mechanically at the beat. The three stresses carry the whole question.
🎤 PART 2 · NOW YOU MARK ONE

Hamlet's last words to Horatio are: "The rest is silence" (Act V, scene 2). Say the line aloud a few times, stressing a different word each time, and listen to what changes.

💬 WHICH WORD CARRIES THE HEAVIEST STRESS?
You want the line to land as a deliberate, final close, not a trailing-off. Which word should you stress hardest?
📓
READING JOURNAL
Mark up the opening four to six lines of the soliloquy you will recite. In your Journal (or on a printed copy), do three things: draw a single slash for a short pause and a double slash for a longer one, wherever the sense pauses; underline the two or three words in each line you will stress; and write one word in the margin for the mood of the passage (grieving, scheming, resolved). Then read it aloud following your own marks. Adjust anything that fights your voice.
💬 ONE MORE REHEARSAL HABIT

Record yourself once this week and listen back before the graded take. You will hear things you cannot feel while performing: a rushed opening, a swallowed ending, a pause that never came. Then record again. The gap between the two takes is where the grade lives.

🤖 WORKING WITH AI

A text-to-speech tool can read your passage aloud so you hear the words, but do not imitate its delivery: synthetic voices flatten exactly the choices, pause, stress, feeling, that a recitation is graded on. Use your own ear and your own reading of the character. The interpretation is the point, and it has to be yours.
