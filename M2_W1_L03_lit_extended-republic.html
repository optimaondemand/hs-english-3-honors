📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Madison's answer is a surprise: to make a republic safer from faction, make it bigger.
Having shown that you cannot cure faction by removing liberty, Madison turns to what you can do: control its effects through the design of the government. Today you finish Federalist No. 10 and follow his two-part cure, the difference between a democracy and a republic, and his counterintuitive claim that a large republic is safer than a small one.

📚 PART 1 · DEMOCRACY VS. REPUBLIC

Madison draws a sharp line between two kinds of popular government. A pure democracy, he writes, is "a society consisting of a small number of citizens, who assemble and administer the government in person" (Federalist 10). Everyone votes directly on everything. Such a system, he argues, offers no cure for faction: if a majority shares a passion, nothing stops it from trampling the minority. A republic, by contrast, is "a government in which the scheme of representation takes place" (Federalist 10). Citizens elect representatives to govern on their behalf. That single move, representation, is the first half of Madison's cure: it passes the people's views through a body of chosen citizens whose wisdom may, in his words, refine and enlarge those views rather than simply obey a passing passion.

📏 PART 2 · THE COUNTERINTUITIVE CURE: MAKE IT BIGGER

Here is Madison's boldest move. Common sense in his day held that a republic had to stay small to survive. Madison argues the opposite: extend the sphere. The larger the republic, the more different interests and groups it contains, and the harder it becomes for any single faction to grow into a majority and seize control. In a small society, one angry group can easily become the whole. In a vast one, a would-be tyrant majority has to combine countless clashing interests, which is far harder to do.

READING JOURNAL
Test Madison's cure against your own country. In four or five sentences: restate, in your own words, why he thinks a large republic is safer from faction, quoting one phrase from No. 10 and citing it. Then take a position: in a nation as large and connected as ours, does "extend the sphere" still protect against a tyranny of the majority, or has size stopped being a safeguard? Argue from Madison's logic, not just your opinion.
🤖 WORKING WITH AI

An AI tool can summarize the difference between a democracy and a republic clearly. But the interesting question here, whether Madison's cure still works at the scale of a modern, media-connected nation, is a matter of judgment that scholars debate. If you ask an AI, expect a confident answer, and treat it as one position to weigh against Madison's actual reasoning, not the last word.
