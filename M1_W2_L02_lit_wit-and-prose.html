📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
The prince who spoke the court's most polished verse now answers every question in prose, and every answer cuts.
In Week 1 you learned to hear blank verse, the five-beat line. Today you learn what it means when Shakespeare turns it off. In the first half of Act II, scene 2, the king recruits two of Hamlet's old friends as watchers, ambassadors bring news from Norway, and Polonius unveils his "mad for love" theory. Then Hamlet enters reading a book, and the play's language changes shape. Your work today: track the shift from verse to prose, and watch Hamlet use wit as both a shield and a blade.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to tell verse from prose on the page and describe what each signals in this play.
2
I will be able to explain how Hamlet's wit works: what a line seems to say to its hearer and what it says to us.
3
I will be able to weigh whether Hamlet's prose is evidence of performance, of genuine disturbance, or of both.
📚 PART 1 · MORE WATCHERS, AND A THEORY

The scene opens with the web from Lesson 2.01 gaining new strands. Claudius welcomes Rosencrantz and Guildenstern, friends of Hamlet's youth. Watch what task he gives them, and how openly he states his purpose in front of the very friends he is using: "To draw him on to pleasures, and to gather / So much as from occasion you may glean" (2.2). Ambassadors also arrive from abroad; notice what news they carry and how quickly, or slowly, the court treats it as settled business. Then Polonius arrives with a theory about Hamlet and evidence he believes proves it, promising the king and queen that "brevity is the soul of wit" (2.2) while being anything but brief. Before you accept his conclusion, notice how the queen responds to the way he presents it: "More matter with less art" (2.2). Watch whether anyone tests the theory before acting on it.

🔗 Read: Hamlet, Act II, scene 2, from the opening through Hamlet's conversation with Rosencrantz and Guildenstern (stop when the players are announced). Folger Shakespeare, Hamlet 2.2.
🎵 PART 2 · THE TECHNIQUE: WHEN VERSE GIVES WAY TO PROSE

Shakespeare's characters do not all speak the same way. Court business, formal occasions, and elevated feeling run in blank verse, the measured five-beat line you learned in Week 1. Prose runs to the margin like ordinary writing, with no line breaks and no fixed beat. In this scene the contrast is a signal you can see on the page. Claudius recruits the spies in smooth verse; the moment Hamlet starts fencing with Polonius, the play drops into prose and mostly stays there while Hamlet is playing mad. The broken register is part of the costume. But listen closely: this "madness" is suspiciously precise. Even Polonius mutters, "Though this be madness, yet there is method in 't" (2.2).

ONE SCENE, TWO REGISTERS · HAMLET 2.2
🏛️ VERSE: THE KING AT WORK
To draw him on to pleasures, and to gather
So much as from occasion you may glean
Lines break at the beat. Each holds close to five stresses. The shape says: official, composed, in control. Even a spying errand sounds stately.
🐺 PROSE: THE PRINCE AT PLAY
"Words, words, words."  ·  "Denmark's a prison."  ·  "there is nothing either good or bad but thinking makes it so"
No line breaks, no fixed beat. The shape says: unofficial, unguarded, maybe unstable. Yet inside the loose form, every reply lands exactly where Hamlet aims it.
On the page, verse is jagged on the right edge; prose runs to the margin. When the register switches, ask who is speaking, to whom, and what mask is on.

⚔️ PART 3 · WIT AS EVASION, WIT AS WEAPON

Hamlet's prose is not filler; it is fencing. When Polonius asks what he reads, the answer, "Words, words, words" (2.2), gives nothing while mocking the question. Calling Polonius a "fishmonger" (2.2) wears madness as a permit to insult. With Rosencrantz and Guildenstern the game turns serious: he corners them into admitting they were sent for, tells them "Denmark's a prison" (2.2), and confides that he has "lost all my mirth" (2.2), so that his great prose hymn, "What a piece of work is a man" (2.2), rises and then collapses into disgust. And just before the players arrive he hands the watchers, and us, a riddle that sounds like a confession: "I am but mad north-north-west. When the wind is southerly, I know a hawk from a handsaw" (2.2). Wit lets Hamlet say true things under cover of nonsense: evasion and attack in the same breath.

🤖 WORKING WITH AI

An AI tool can tell you which passages of a Shakespeare scene are verse and which are prose, and it is usually right, because the difference is visible in the text's line breaks. What it cannot decide is why the register shifts at a given moment, since the play never says. It may also misquote: Hamlet's lines exist in slightly different versions across editions. Check any quotation against the edition you are actually reading before it goes in your Journal.
