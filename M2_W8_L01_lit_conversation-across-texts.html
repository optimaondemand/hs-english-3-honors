📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Three Americans, three answers to the same question. Your op-ed begins when you decide what the question is.
You have spent seven weeks with three writers who never met and never stopped arguing. Publius trusts structure: the state governs itself through checks and design. Emerson trusts conscience: the soul governs itself or it is not free at all. Douglass is the evidence both of them needed: a self seized back, by force of literacy and will, from a nation that denied it everything. Today you seat the three of them around one table, choose the live civic question your op-ed will answer, and map what each voice supplies you: evidence, a counter-position, or both.

🏛️ PART 1 · THREE VOICES, ONE QUESTION

Here is the method in miniature. Take one question the whole module has circled: when a law seems unjust, what should a free person do? Now listen to each writer answer it in turn. Read down each column to hear one voice whole, then across each row to hear the argument between them. Notice that every voice is strong somewhere, and every voice has a hard question waiting for it. Your op-ed will do this same work with the question you choose.

ONE QUESTION, THREE VOICES: WHEN A LAW SEEMS UNJUST, WHAT SHOULD A FREE PERSON DO?
PUBLIUS
The Federalist (1787–88): the state governs itself		 EMERSON
"Self-Reliance" (1841): the soul governs itself		 DOUGLASS
Narrative (1845): the self seized back
THE ANSWER Work within the structure. No one may be judge in his own cause; a government must "control the governed" and then "control itself" (Federalist 51). Change the law through the process built for changing laws. Answer to your own conscience first. "Nothing is at last sacred but the integrity of your own mind" ("Self-Reliance"). A soul that waits for permission to do right has already surrendered. Ask who the law serves before you honor it. The law he lived under made teaching a child to read a crime; the ban itself showed him "the pathway from slavery to freedom" (Narrative, ch. 6).
THE EVIDENCE Reasoning from human nature: "the latent causes of faction are thus sown in the nature of man" (Federalist 10), so "Ambition must be made to counteract ambition" (Federalist 51). Argument by design. Appeal to the reader's own experience of conformity: "The virtue in most request is conformity" ("Self-Reliance"). Argument by provocation, daring you to check his claim against your life. Lived fact, remembered exactly: the forbidden alphabet, the stolen lessons, the fight with Covey. "You have seen how a man was made a slave; you shall see how a slave was made a man" (Narrative, ch. 10).
THE HARD QUESTION FOR THIS VOICE What does the citizen owe a process that is procedurally valid and morally wrong? Douglass lived under exactly such laws, and the process offered him nothing. If every conscience is its own final court, what stops a million sincere consciences from becoming the very faction Publius feared, each "united and actuated by some common impulse of passion" (Federalist 10)? One life is the strongest evidence and the smallest sample. What principle turns his single testimony into a rule for the republic, and who builds the structure that enforces it?

This is what "putting texts in conversation" means in practice. You do not summarize three writers in three separate paragraphs. You let them answer the same question, agree where they honestly agree, and collide where they honestly collide, and then you take your own position inside that tension.

🗳️ PART 2 · CHOOSING A LIVE CIVIC QUESTION

Your op-ed needs a question that is alive right now, not a settled one. The module's three great tensions are your hunting grounds. Under each one, real questions are being argued in legislatures, school boards, courtrooms, and kitchen tables this year:

CONSCIENCE VS. LAW
Should citizens ever break a law they believe is unjust, or only work to change it? Should professionals be required to provide services their conscience objects to? When is protest that disrupts public life justified?
THE INDIVIDUAL VS. THE INSTITUTION
Should platforms or governments decide what speech is acceptable online? Should schools be able to restrict what students say or wear? How much power should any one branch, agency, or company hold over daily life?
SELF-RELIANCE VS. OBLIGATION
Should voting be required by law? Should young people owe a term of national or community service? What do the successful owe the communities that raised them, and should that debt ever be legislated?

Choose from these or bring your own, but test your question against four checks before you commit. First, it is genuinely contested: informed, decent people currently disagree. Second, all three texts can speak to it: Publius has a structural angle, Emerson a conscience angle, Douglass a lived-evidence angle, even if one of them ends up on the other side of your argument. Third, scholars have written about it, because Week 2 taught you to search JStor and your op-ed must carry researched support. Fourth, you care about the answer, because 700 words of pretended conviction reads exactly like what it is.

⚖️ PART 3 · ASSIGN THE VOICES THEIR SEATS

Once the question is chosen, give each writer a job. One or two of them will argue your side: they supply evidence, a quotation or an episode that carries weight your bare opinion cannot. At least one of them will push against you: he supplies your counter-position, the strongest objection your op-ed must concede and answer. Do not force all three onto your side; an op-ed that conscripts every witness convinces no one, and the revision lesson later this week will hunt for exactly that flattening. The writer who disagrees with you is the most useful one at the table, because answering him honestly is what makes a reader trust you.

📓
READING JOURNAL
Build your own three-voice table in your Module 2 Organizer, on your chosen civic question. Write the question at the top in one sentence that ends with a question mark. Then give each writer two rows: THE ANSWER he would give, in your own words, and THE EVIDENCE you would cite from his text, quoted briefly and cited the way this module always cites: (Federalist 10 or 51), ("Self-Reliance"), (Narrative, ch. 6). Finish by marking each column FOR ME or AGAINST ME. Coaching: run the four checks from Part 2 before you fill anything in, and if a column sits empty because a writer has nothing to say to your question, that is your signal to sharpen the question, not to skip the voice.
🤖 WORKING WITH AI

Asked to compare three thinkers, AI tools tend to produce a smooth "all three value freedom" summary that erases the collisions your op-ed is built on. An AI also cannot tell you which civic question you actually care about, and that choice decides whether the whole project works. If you use AI here, limit it to checking a quotation's exact wording or a date, then build the table and choose the question yourself.
