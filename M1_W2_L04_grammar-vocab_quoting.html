📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A quotation dropped into a paragraph sits there like a stone. A quotation woven in becomes part of your own sentence's muscle.
All week your Journal tasks have asked you to quote the play, and every claim you post in this week's discussion needs lines behind it. So today's grammar is the mechanics of doing that cleanly: how to integrate a quotation into your own sentence and how to punctuate the seam so no reader can see the stitching. Then two words from Act II for your Word Study.

1
I will be able to weave a quoted phrase into my own sentence so the grammar of the whole still works.
2

✏️ PART 1 · THREE WAYS TO BRING A QUOTATION IN

There are three standard ways to attach quoted words to your own, and the punctuation follows from which one you choose. Woven: the quoted phrase serves as a grammatical part of your sentence (a noun, an object, a description), and you use no extra punctuation before it. Speech tag: a short introducing clause like Hamlet says or Polonius insists takes a comma before the quotation. Colon setup: a complete sentence of your own, followed by a colon, then the quotation standing as evidence. The test is always the same: read the sentence aloud with the quotation marks removed. If it sounds like one grammatical sentence, the seam is clean. After the quotation, cite act and scene in parentheses: (2.2).

ONE LINE, THREE CLEAN SEAMS · AND ONE BROKEN ONE
✗ Dropped in (avoid): Hamlet mocks Polonius. "Words, words, words" (2.2). The quotation floats alone; no sentence of yours holds it.
↓
Woven (no punctuation): Hamlet deflates Polonius's question by reducing his book to "words, words, words" (2.2).
Speech tag (comma): Baffled but impressed, Polonius admits, "Though this be madness, yet there is method in 't" (2.2).
Colon setup (colon): The act ends with a plan, not a feeling: "The play's the thing / Wherein I'll catch the conscience of the King" (2.2).
The shaded spots mark the seam. Notice the last example keeps the slash ( / ) where the verse line breaks; keep it whenever you quote more than one line of verse inside your paragraph.

Two more mechanics worth locking in now. First, if you trim or adjust a quoted word so it fits your grammar, show the change in square brackets: Hamlet swears the actor "would drown the stage with tears" while he himself remains "unpregnant of [his] cause" (2.2). Second, quote the smallest piece that proves your point; a few exact words inside a strong sentence of yours beat four lines copied out.

READING JOURNAL
Take Hamlet's line "Denmark's a prison" (2.2) and write it into your Journal three times, once in each pattern: woven into your own sentence with no punctuation at the seam, introduced by a speech tag with a comma, and set up by a complete sentence with a colon. Then read each aloud without the quotation marks and mark the one that flows best. Keep the citation (2.2) after every version. You will use your favorite pattern in this week's discussion post.
📕 PART 2 · WORD STUDY (from Act II)

Two words from Act II, defined where they appear.

WordIn the playWhat it meansWord origin
brevity
noun		 Polonius promises the king and queen that "brevity is the soul of wit" (2.2), then talks on at great length. Shortness in speech or writing; saying a thing in few words. From Latin brevitas, from brevis ("short"); the same root gives "brief" and "abbreviate," all carrying the plain sense of shortness.
pestilent
adjective		 Hamlet tells his old friends the very air has become "a foul and pestilent congregation of vapors" (2.2). Spreading disease or harm; poisonous, deadly to health or spirit. From Latin pestilentem, from pestis ("plague, deadly disease"); a word for fatal epidemic came to describe anything poisonous or corrupting.
📓
READING JOURNAL · WORD STUDY
Add both words to the Word Study section of your Journal. For each, write one new sentence of your own on a subject that has nothing to do with Hamlet, keeping the meaning exact. For example, for brevity: "The coach's halftime speech was ten seconds long, and its brevity said more than a lecture could." Aim to make the sentence show the meaning, not just contain the word. Notice how pestilent's root sense of literal plague still echoes when we call an idea or influence "pestilent."
