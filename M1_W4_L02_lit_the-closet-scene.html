📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A son comes to his mother's room to speak daggers. A voice cries out behind the curtain, and he uses a real one.
In Lesson 4.01, Hamlet stood over the king with every advantage and reasoned his way out of striking. Now, in his mother's private chamber, with no time to reason at all, he strikes. The blade goes through the arras, the hanging tapestry, and into Polonius, who had hidden there to listen. Today you read the first half of Act III, scene 4, and study what one rash act does to a plot: this is the play's point of no return, the moment after which nothing can be walked back.

📚 PART 1 · THE RAT BEHIND THE ARRAS

Remember how this trap was set. At the end of scene 3, Polonius told the king he would hide behind the arras in the queen's chamber to overhear Hamlet, one more act of the spying you have tracked since Act II. The scene opens with mother and son trading the same sentence like a blow returned: "Hamlet, thou hast thy father much offended," she begins, meaning Claudius. "Mother, you have my father much offended," he answers, meaning the dead king (3.4). One word swapped, two different fathers, and the whole quarrel is already on the table.

Hamlet is rougher than she expects. He forces her to sit: "You go not till I set you up a glass / Where you may see the inmost part of you" (3.4). He means a mirror, but Gertrude hears a threat and cries out: "What wilt thou do? Thou wilt not murder me? / Help, ho!" (3.4). Behind the arras, Polonius echoes her cry for help. And Hamlet, who deliberated for a full speech over the kneeling king, does not deliberate at all. "How now, a rat? Dead for a ducat, dead" (3.4). One thrust through the cloth. His first question tells you everything about what he hoped: "Is it the King?" (3.4). It is not. His farewell to the body is a verdict on the man and, though he does not hear it yet, on himself: "Thou wretched, rash, intruding fool, farewell" (3.4). Rash is exactly the word; it has simply changed owners.

Then the interrogation Hamlet came for. He answers his mother's shock at the killing by naming a worse deed, "almost as bad, good mother, / As kill a king and marry with his brother" (3.4), and her reply, "As kill a king?" (3.4), is worth pausing over: her surprise is one of the play's few clues about how much she knew. He holds up the two portraits, his father and his uncle, "The counterfeit presentment of two brothers" (3.4), and makes her look from one to the other. It works. She sees herself: "Thou turn'st my eyes into my very soul, / And there I see such black and grainèd spots" (3.4), and begs him to stop: "These words like daggers enter in my ears" (3.4). Daggers were his stated plan for this visit; the play lets you hear the metaphor land where the sword just did.

🔁 PART 2 · THE TECHNIQUE: THE POINT OF NO RETURN

Plots have a hinge: a single action after which the story can only run downhill in a new direction. Before this moment, Hamlet is a wronged man who has done no wrong; every option is still open to him. One thrust through a curtain closes them. He meant it for the king, so the act is attempted revenge; it kills an old fool, so the act is also a blunder; and either way it is a killing he cannot take back. Watch what one rash second sets in motion.

CONSEQUENCE SET IN MOTION

Set the two halves of this week side by side and the design is hard to miss. In 3.3, Hamlet has certainty, opportunity, and time to think, and the thinking stops his hand. In 3.4, he has a muffled cry and no time at all, and the hand moves before the thought. The play is asking a hard question about deliberation itself: if his reason spares the guilty man and his instinct kills an innocent one, what exactly is all that thinking for?

🏛️ A QUESTION WORTH SITTING WITH: WHAT KIND OF ACT IS THIS?

It is worth pausing on what actually happens here, apart from what it sets in motion. Hamlet hears a voice behind a curtain and drives a sword through it without knowing who stands there. His own first question afterward, "Is it the King?" (3.4), tells you he was hoping, not certain. He kills a man he cannot see, and the man turns out to be an old counselor, not the king he meant to strike. Readers have long argued over what this moment says about Hamlet as a hero: is this decisive courage finally breaking through his hesitation, or a rash, reckless act that happens to land on the wrong person? Both readings can point to the same lines. He does not check who is there before he strikes, and "rash" is the very word he uses for Polonius while pulling the sword free, a word that could just as easily describe his own thrust.

READING JOURNAL
Write three or four sentences on the question "Is it the King?" (3.4). To do it well: say what the question proves about Hamlet's intent when he stabbed, then set this instant kill against the sparing in 3.3, where he had the real king in front of him and waited. End with one sentence on what the pairing suggests: is Hamlet's problem an unwillingness to act, or something stranger about when he can act? Quote both scenes; cite by act and scene.
🤖 WORKING WITH AI

An AI tool can list the consequences of Polonius's death across the rest of the play, and its list will look like the diagram above. What it cannot supply is the weight of the moment as you read it: the speed of the stab after a full scene of deliberation. Summaries flatten pacing, and pacing is the argument here. Read the lines in order yourself before you let anything summarize them for you.
