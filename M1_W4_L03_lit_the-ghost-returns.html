📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
The dead king walks into his wife's chamber. His son sees him. His wife sees nothing at all.
In Act I, the Ghost appeared on the battlements and four different people saw it. Now it appears again, mid-quarrel, in Gertrude's chamber, and only Hamlet can see or hear it. Today you finish Act III and take up the question this staging forces: is the Ghost a real visitor from beyond the play's world, or a projection of Hamlet's overdriven mind? Readers and directors have split on this for four hundred years, and the text keeps the door open in both directions. Your job is not to settle it. Your job is to see exactly which lines hold it open.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…

I will be able to state why the Ghost says it has returned, citing its own words.
2

3
I will be able to build a two-sided evidence case for an open question without forcing a verdict the text does not give.
📚 PART 1 · "TO WHET THY ALMOST BLUNTED PURPOSE"

Hamlet is at the peak of his fury, forcing his mother to compare the two portraits, when he suddenly stops and stares past her. The Ghost has entered. His first instinct is guilt: he asks whether it has come "your tardy son to chide" (3.4), the son who let time pass and let the purpose cool. The Ghost confirms it: "Do not forget. This visitation / Is but to whet thy almost blunted purpose" (3.4). To whet is to sharpen a blade; the revenge, it says, has gone dull. But notice its second concern, and its gentleness: "But look, amazement on thy mother sits" (3.4). It tells Hamlet to step between her and her fear, to speak to her. Even here, the Ghost's charge from Act I holds its old shape: strike the king, spare the queen.

Then the scene splits in two. Hamlet speaks to the figure; Gertrude watches her son speak to an empty room. "Alas, he's mad" (3.4), she concludes. He points: "On him, on him! Look you how pale he glares" (3.4). She asks what he is looking at, and her answer to his "Do you see nothing there?" is one of the most carefully balanced lines in the play: "Nothing at all; yet all that is I see" (3.4). She sees everything present, and the Ghost is not among it. She names his vision "the very coinage of your brain," a thing minted by madness, "This bodiless creation ecstasy / Is very cunning in" (3.4). Ecstasy here means madness, a mind standing outside itself; hold onto the word, it is in this week's Word Study.

Hamlet's defense is precise: "My pulse as yours doth temperately keep time / And makes as healthful music. It is not madness / That I have uttered" (3.4). Offer me any test, he says, and I will reword the whole matter, "which madness / Would gambol from" (3.4): a mad mind would skip away from the retelling, and his does not. Then he turns preacher. He urges her to own the past and refuse the future: "Confess yourself to heaven, / Repent what's past, avoid what is to come" (3.4), to stay out of Claudius's bed, and, if the feeling is not yet real, to act the better part until it becomes real: "Assume a virtue if you have it not" (3.4). His summary of his own harshness could stand over the whole scene: "I must be cruel only to be kind" (3.4). He leaves dragging Polonius out, already knowing the price of the night's work: "I must to England, you know that" (3.4).

👁️ PART 2 · THE TECHNIQUE: PERSPECTIVE AND RELIABILITY

Everything you know in a play arrives through someone's perspective, and perspectives can be tested for reliability the way a court tests a witness: how many saw it, in what state, and with what stake in the answer. Scene 4 makes the test unavoidable, because two sane-sounding witnesses in the same room give opposite testimony about the same figure. The play could easily have resolved this; a single line from Gertrude, or a second witness, would do it. Shakespeare withholds that line. Weigh both files below, and notice that each side has real evidence, not just a mood.

THE GHOST IN 3.4: TWO CASE FILES, ONE OPEN VERDICT
CASE FILE A · A REAL VISITOR
• In Act I it was seen by Barnardo, Marcellus, and Horatio before Hamlet ever knew of it (1.1).
• Its account of the murder has since been confirmed: Claudius's guilty aside (3.1), his fleeing the Mousetrap (3.2), his confession at prayer (3.3).
• Hamlet offers a test on the spot: his pulse keeps time "temperately," and he can retell everything "which madness / Would gambol from" (3.4).
CASE FILE B · A PROJECTION
• This time, only Hamlet sees or hears it; Gertrude, looking straight at the spot, sees "Nothing at all" (3.4).
• She has a ready diagnosis: "the very coinage of your brain," the work of "ecstasy" (3.4).
• It appears at the exact moment Hamlet's fury at his mother outruns the Ghost's Act I command to leave her to heaven, as if something in him called himself back to the task.
The text supports both files and closes neither. A director must choose; a reader gets to hold both. When a question is genuinely open, the strong move is not picking faster, it is citing better.

READING JOURNAL
Build your own two-column page for the Ghost question. In one column, list the two strongest pieces of textual evidence you can find that the Ghost is real; in the other, the two strongest that it is a projection. Quote each and cite by act and scene, drawing on Act I as well as 3.4. Then, below the columns, write two sentences: which single piece of evidence do you find hardest to argue away, and what would the play lose if Shakespeare had settled the question for you? There is no right verdict here; the quality of the citations is the work.

🤖 WORKING WITH AI

Ask an AI tool whether the Ghost is real and it will usually give you a tidy answer, sometimes with invented citations to critics. The honest state of the question is that the text sustains both readings, and scholars still disagree. An AI can help you locate the relevant lines faster; it cannot relieve you of the judgment, and on open questions it tends to manufacture closure. Keep the question open on purpose; that is the skill this lesson is teaching.
