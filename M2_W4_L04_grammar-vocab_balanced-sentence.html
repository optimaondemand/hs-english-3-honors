The Balanced Sentence
Lesson · one combined grammar and vocabulary session
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Emerson's sentences stick in the memory because so many of them are built like scales: two matched pans, one pivot.
In Module 1 you learned the semicolon as a hinge between two independent clauses. Today you learn the sentence form that puts that hinge to its highest use: the balanced sentence, two clauses of matching grammatical shape weighed against each other across a semicolon. It is the natural form for comparison, which makes it exactly the tool this week's Madison-and-Emerson quick-write needs.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to build a balanced sentence: two independent clauses of matching shape joined at a semicolon.
2
I will be able to check the two tests every balanced sentence must pass: both sides stand alone, and both sides match in structure.
3
I will be able to use two words from "Self-Reliance" precisely in my own sentences.
✏️ PART 1 · TWO PANS, ONE PIVOT

A balanced sentence sets two independent clauses side by side and gives them the same grammatical shape, so the reader weighs one against the other. The semicolon is the pivot of the scale. You already know the semicolon rule from Module 1: both sides must be able to stand alone as complete sentences. Balance adds a second requirement: the two sides should mirror each other, subject for subject, verb for verb, so that whatever differs stands out sharply. Emerson wrote one of the cleanest examples in American prose.

THE FRAME (EMERSON'S OWN SENTENCE)
It is easy in the world to live after the world's opinion;it is easy in solitude to live after our own ("Self-Reliance")
Blue and gold are twins: it is easy + a place + to live after + whose opinion. Because everything else matches, the two differences (bolded) carry the whole meaning: world vs. solitude, the world's opinion vs. our own. The semicolon is the pivot; each pan of the scale could stand alone as a sentence. Emerson then breaks the pattern on purpose with a third clause beginning "but the great man…", and the break lands hard precisely because the balance came first.

To build your own, write the first clause, then rebuild it with the terms swapped: Madison trusts the structure of the republic; Emerson trusts the structure of the soul. Run both tests before you keep it. Stand-alone test: cover each side of the semicolon and check that what remains is a complete sentence. Mirror test: line the clauses up and check that each part has a partner of the same kind. Two cautions. First, if the second half only completes the first (Madison trusts structure; especially checks and balances), the second pan is a fragment and the semicolon is wrong. Second, balance is a spice, not a diet: one balanced sentence gives a paragraph its spine, while three in a row sound like a fortune cookie.

📕 PART 2 · WORD STUDY (from "Self-Reliance")
WordIn the textWhat it meansWord origin
aversion
noun		 Right after the joint-stock sentence, Emerson writes: "The virtue in most request is conformity. Self-reliance is its aversion" ("Self-Reliance"). A settled, strong dislike; the thing one turns away from. Emerson means society does not merely prefer conformity, it actively recoils from self-reliance. From Latin aversionem, from avertere ("to turn away"), from ab- ("away") and vertere ("to turn"); literally a turning away from something disliked.
suffrage
noun		 "Absolve you to yourself, and you shall have the suffrage of the world" ("Self-Reliance"). Approval, support, or consent; in modern civic use, the right to vote. Emerson uses the older sense: clear your conscience before yourself, and the world's approval will follow. From Latin suffragium ("a vote, support"); the deeper root is uncertain, possibly linked to frangere ("to break," from broken pottery pieces once used as ballots).
📓
READING JOURNAL · WORD STUDY
Add both words to your Journal. For each, write one new sentence of your own on a subject unrelated to Emerson, keeping the meaning exact and making the sentence show the meaning. For aversion, name both the person who feels it and the thing it turns away from; "an aversion to" is the usual pattern. For suffrage, pick one sense and commit: either approval and support (Emerson's sense) or the right to vote (the sense you will meet in Douglass's century), and make your sentence leave no doubt which one you mean. Bonus move: cast one of the two sentences as a balanced sentence from Part 1. Notice how aversion's root sense of "turning away" still echoes in the modern word.
