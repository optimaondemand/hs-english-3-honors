📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Unelected judges, appointed for life. To readers who had just overthrown a king, that sounded like aristocracy. Hamilton's answer: they are the weakest branch you have.
Today the pen name Publius passes from Madison to Alexander Hamilton, and the subject turns to the branch the ratification debate trusted least: the courts. In Federalist No. 78 Hamilton takes his readers' sharpest fear and argues it inside out. Watch how he does it; turning an objection into your own best evidence is a move you will want for your op-ed.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain why Hamilton calls the judiciary the least dangerous branch.
2
I will be able to define judicial review and explain why Hamilton says it keeps the people, not the judges, supreme.
3
I will be able to explain how permanent tenure is meant to protect judicial independence.
📚 PART 1 · NO SWORD, NO PURSE

Hamilton opens by meeting the fear head on. Compare what each branch actually holds. The executive commands the military: the sword. The legislature controls taxing and spending: the purse. The courts hold neither. They cannot fund anything, arrest anyone, or field an army; they can only hear cases and give reasons. That is why Hamilton claims "the judiciary, from the nature of its functions, will always be the least dangerous to the political rights of the Constitution" (Federalist 78). His summary line is the sharpest antithesis in the essay: "It may truly be said to have neither FORCE nor WILL, but merely judgment" (Federalist 78). The capitals are Hamilton's own emphasis; force belongs to the executive, will belongs to the legislature, and the courts get only judgment.

📜 PART 2 · JUDICIAL REVIEW: WHO OUTRANKS WHOM?

If the courts are so weak, what are they for? Hamilton's answer is the idea we now call judicial review. The Constitution is fundamental law, the people's own act, standing above any ordinary statute. When Congress passes a law that clashes with it, someone must say which prevails, and Hamilton assigns the courts that duty: "to declare all acts contrary to the manifest tenor of the Constitution void" (Federalist 78). He knows the objection is coming: does this not set judges above the elected legislature? No, he argues, it sets the people above both. Congress is the people's servant, and a servant's orders cannot outrank the master's standing instructions. When a court voids an unconstitutional act, it is not preferring its own will to the lawmakers'; it is preferring the people's Constitution to the lawmakers' overreach.

🔒 PART 3 · INDEPENDENCE, BY PERMANENT TENURE

A duty that heavy needs protection. A judge who must please politicians to keep the job will start ruling like a politician; a judge who must please the crowd will bend with the crowd, even when the crowd is being stirred against someone's rights. So the Constitution gives federal judges permanent tenure: they hold office during good behavior, not for a term, removable only by impeachment for misconduct. Hamilton defends the arrangement directly: "nothing will contribute so much as this to that independent spirit in the judges which must be essential to the faithful performance of so arduous a duty" (Federalist 78). Standing against a popular but unconstitutional law takes fortitude, and Hamilton's wager is that security in office is what makes such fortitude possible. Notice the trade he is offering his readers: give up some accountability at the ballot box, and gain a guardian who cannot be threatened into silence.

🔗 Read: The Federalist No. 78 (the whole essay; it is shorter than it looks, and you now know its three moves). Public-domain text: The Avalon Project, Yale Law School.

READING JOURNAL
Hamilton's argument stands on three legs: least dangerous, judicial review, and independence through tenure. In four or five sentences, explain how the three depend on each other; for example, why judicial review would be alarming without the "merely judgment" point, or why tenure matters only if courts really have the duty to void laws. Quote one of this lesson's Federalist 78 lines and cite it. Strong answers connect the legs instead of listing them; check that at least one of your sentences begins with "Without…" or "Because…".
🎤 RECITATION CHECKPOINT · PATRICK HENRY (UNGRADED)

While the Federalist argued in print, founding-era persuasion also lived in the voice. This quarter's graded recitation is a Douglass passage in Week 7; this week is your first rehearsal checkpoint on the way there, using the most famous spoken argument of the Revolution: Patrick Henry's 1775 "Give Me Liberty" speech.

Do this now, nothing to submit: open the speech (public-domain text: The Avalon Project, Yale Law School), choose a passage of roughly six to ten lines that ends with a strong final sentence, and read it aloud once, standing, at full voice. As you read, mark two places where you would pause and one word in each sentence that deserves the most weight. Keep your marked copy; you will use the same annotation method for Douglass.

Listen for what the page cannot show: Henry builds pressure through repeated questions and short, hammering clauses. Where does your voice naturally rise? That is rhetoric working on you from the inside.

🤖 WORKING WITH AI

An AI tool can explain judicial review and summarize No. 78 competently. Two cautions. First, quotations: AI frequently smooths Hamilton's wording, and this essay's force lives in exact phrases like "neither FORCE nor WILL," so quote from the text itself. Second, the question of whether today's courts still fit Hamilton's "least dangerous" description is actively debated; an AI will give you one confident-sounding position, and you should treat it as one voice in that debate, not the verdict.
