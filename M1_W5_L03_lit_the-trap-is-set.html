📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A king takes a grieving son by the shoulder, agrees with everything he feels, and hands him a poisoned sword.
Hamlet is coming back. A letter reaches Horatio in scene 6: the prince was taken by pirates at sea and is returning to Denmark alone, without his escorts. Claudius hears the same news mid-conversation with Laertes, and scene 7 is what he does about it: one of the most chilling stretches of persuasion in the play. Today you finish Act IV, watch a master manipulator turn grief into a weapon, and complete the set of foils the play has been building around Hamlet all week.

📚 PART 1 · HOW TO AIM ANOTHER MAN'S GRIEF

Watch Claudius work, because the scene is a masterclass in manipulation and every move is visible on the page. First he absorbs the rage: by the time scene 7 opens he has already convinced Laertes that Hamlet alone is to blame and that the king is his friend. Then, when word arrives that Hamlet is back in Denmark, Claudius does something remarkable: he questions the sincerity of Laertes's grief to his face. Not cruelly; clinically. Love, he says, is "begun by time," and time cools it: "Time qualifies the spark and fire of it" (4.7). Then the sentence that could stand over the whole play: "that we would do, / We should do when we would" (4.7), because would changes, and delay kills resolve. Read that line twice. It is Hamlet's entire tragedy, diagnosed exactly, by the villain, as a sales pitch. Claudius has watched delay defeat his enemy for four acts, and now he inoculates his new weapon against it.

Then comes the plan, and notice who supplies which part. Claudius proposes a friendly fencing exhibition in which Laertes may "choose / A sword unbated" (4.7), a blade with no safety tip, and kill Hamlet in what will look like an accident. Laertes does not hesitate; he improves it: "I'll anoint my sword. / I bought an unction of a mountebank" (4.7), a poison so strong a scratch will kill. And Claudius adds the third layer: "A chalice for the nonce, whereon but sipping" (4.7), a poisoned cup waiting in reserve if the blade should miss. Count the safeguards: a rigged weapon, a poisoned edge, a poisoned drink. The king who once knelt failing to pray now engineers a murder in triplicate, and the grieving son who was ready to cut Hamlet's throat in a church has agreed to do it by trickery instead, in a match built on the pretense of friendship. Ask what each man has traded away to get here.

⚔️ PART 2 · THE FULL SET OF FOILS: THREE SONS, THREE ANSWERS

In Lesson 5.01 you learned the foil: a character placed beside another so the contrast sharpens both. Act IV completes the design. Shakespeare has now shown you three sons, each with a father violently dead, each owing that father an answer. Fortinbras turns his claim into open, disciplined war over a trifle; Laertes turns his into instant, lawless vengeance that a smarter man redirects within a single scene; Hamlet, whose cause is the greatest of the three, has examined his all the way to a standstill. No one of them is the play's answer. Fortinbras acts without enough reason; Laertes acts without any conscience; Hamlet has conscience and reason in such supply that action keeps dying in committee. The three of them together are the play's real question: what would it look like to act rightly, with both eyes open?

Laertes is worth a second look for what he shares with Hamlet, not just how he differs. He is a second revenger, a son of the same court answering a father's death, and for a moment the play runs Hamlet's own errand through someone else's hands, at another man's speed. Watching what Laertes is willing to trade away, allegiance, damnation, patience, to reach Hamlet is one way of watching what Hamlet has refused to trade, and asking whether that refusal is conscience or something closer to paralysis.

THREE SONS, THREE ANSWERS TO A DEAD FATHER
ACTS ON THE THINNEST CAUSE
Fortinbras
Marches twenty thousand men for "a little patch of ground" (4.4). Method: open war, under license, in his own name.
ACTS WITHOUT CONSCIENCE
Laertes
"I dare damnation" (4.5); by scene 7, an anointed blade and a borrowed plot. Method: instant revenge, steered by a king.
EXAMINES, AND WAITS
Hamlet
"Cause, and will, and strength, and means" (4.4), and still no deed. Method: test everything; the testing becomes the delay.
Read across the row: cause shrinks as speed grows. Hamlet has the most reason and the least motion. The play never says which son is right; it makes you weigh them.
🌷 PART 3 · THE WILLOW SPEECH: HOW ACT IV ENDS

The plotting is interrupted by the queen, carrying the act's last news: Ophelia has drowned. Gertrude's report begins "There is a willow grows askant the brook" (4.7) and unfolds like a painting: the garlands of wildflowers, a broken branch, the girl borne up for a while by her spread clothes, singing scraps of old tunes, until "her garments, heavy with their drink" (4.7) pulled her down "To muddy death" (4.7). Notice what Shakespeare chooses here. The death happens offstage, and we receive it as a set piece of deliberate, unhurried beauty, spoken by the queen, in the same room where two men have just finished designing a murder. Ask the scene the questions it invites and does not answer. How does Gertrude know these details, and why does no one who watched intervene? Was the drowning an accident of madness or something chosen? The play keeps every one of those doors open, and the gravediggers will argue the last one in Act V. What is certain is the design: Act IV opened with one of Polonius's children destroyed by Hamlet's sword-thrust, and it closes with the other, while the man responsible sails home unaware of any of it.

READING JOURNAL
Claudius never draws a weapon in this act, yet he is its most dangerous figure. In your Journal, write four or five sentences on how he turns Laertes's grief to his own use in 4.7. To do it well: quote the single line of his persuasion you find most effective and cite it (4.7); name the move it makes (agreeing with the grief, questioning it, or urging speed); then note one thing Laertes gives up by accepting the king's plan instead of the open revenge he stormed in with. Add a line to your evolving answer to the module question: what does this act suggest happens when action is not governed by conscience?
🤖 WORKING WITH AI

An AI tool can list the steps of the fencing plot accurately; the scene is famous and well documented. What it cannot do is weigh the open questions the text refuses to close, such as whether Ophelia's drowning was accident or choice, or how much Gertrude understands as she reports it. If you ask, an AI will often pick one reading and state it as settled. The text is the authority here, and the text is deliberately silent. When you write about this act, mark clearly which claims the lines support and which are your inference.
