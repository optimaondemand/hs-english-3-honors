🎓 HONORS-ONLY EXTENSION
This week is built for English III Honors students only. Both English III and English III Honors complete the same eight-week Module 2 base, Weeks 1 through 8, and standard English III finishes the module at Week 8 with the graded Publius Returns final. Week 9 is enrichment layered on top of that shared base: nothing here is required of, or available to, standard-course students, and nothing in the standard course depends on it.
WEEK 9 MAP
The Fuller Conversation
You have already finished Publius Returns. This week you do what a serious writer does after the draft is done: you stop admiring your own argument and start attacking it. First you find the strongest objection to your own claim, not a weak one you can wave away. Then you widen the table, bringing a fourth voice, the nation's founding self-evident truths, into the conversation Publius, Emerson, and Douglass have been having all module, and see how it sharpens or complicates everything you have argued. Finally you write the reply your own harshest honest critic would write. There is no new reading this week; you are working with the texts you already know, at a deeper level.
🎯 THE QUESTION BEHIND MODULE 2
How do free people order their common life, how does a free person order an inner one, and what happens when a nation denies that self-government to some of its people?
This week you test how well your own answer to that question holds up when someone else pushes back on it, and what a fourth voice, one you have not yet let speak, does to the conversation.

1
Lesson 2.32: The Strongest Objection (enrichment lesson)
See how Publius, Emerson, and Douglass each anticipate the hardest objection to their own case, and answer it in their own words. Then find the strongest objection to your op-ed.

Lesson 2.33: Widening the Circle (enrichment lesson)
Bring a fourth voice, the Declaration's claim that "all men are created equal," into the conversation among Publius, Emerson, and Douglass, and see what it changes.
3
The Anti-Publius (completion, 15 pts, Word doc)
Write the strongest good-faith rebuttal to your own Publius Returns op-ed, under a new pseudonym: concede what is fair, then press the hardest counterargument.
