📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
The prince who could not act now walks knowingly toward a rigged duel, and he is calm.
Hamlet comes back from the sea a different man. He tells Horatio how he turned the king's death-warrant against the men carrying it, and he speaks about fate with a settled calm we have not heard before. Today you read the first half of the final scene and study that change: the move from endless questioning to a strange readiness.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to describe how Hamlet escaped the voyage and what it shows about his new decisiveness.
2
I will be able to explain what Hamlet means by "the readiness is all" and how it differs from his earlier paralysis.
3
I will be able to weigh whether Hamlet's calm is wisdom, surrender, or both.
📚 PART 1 · THE MAN WHO CAME BACK

Hamlet tells Horatio what happened at sea. Unable to sleep, on impulse he found the sealed commission he was carrying, opened it, and read his own death order: England was to behead him on arrival. He did not agonize. He wrote a new order condemning the bearers, Rosencrantz and Guildenstern, sealed it with his father's signet ring, and slipped it back. This is the man who spent three acts unable to strike acting instantly and without a second thought. He explains it as more than cleverness: "There's a divinity that shapes our ends, / Rough-hew them how we will" (5.2). Something larger than his own planning, he now believes, is shaping events; his part is to be ready when the moment comes.

⚖️ A HARDER LOOK: HAMLET AND HIS CHILDHOOD FRIENDS

Notice what Hamlet says almost in passing here. The commission he rewrote at sea did not just save his own life; it sent Rosencrantz and Guildenstern, two courtiers he grew up with, to be executed in England in his place. He does not agonize over it. "They are not near my conscience," he tells Horatio; "their defeat / Does by their own insinuation grow" (5.2). His reasoning is that they knew whose errand they were running and put themselves between him and a king plotting his death. That is a real argument: they were, in their way, a threat to his life. It is also true that the same Hamlet who spent three acts unable to strike even his father's actual murderer dispatches two former friends without a second thought once his own life is at stake. Whatever else this Act V calm is, it includes quick, lethal self-preservation, and that complicates any simple reading of him as a straightforward hero. Hold both facts at once as you weigh him.

💭 WEIGH IT (no single right answer)
Hamlet says of their deaths, "They are not near my conscience" (5.2). Given everything you have read of him, how do you weigh that line?
⚖️ PART 2 · FROM QUESTION TO READINESS

The foppish courtier Osric arrives with a wager: the king has bet on a fencing match between Hamlet and Laertes. Horatio, uneasy, offers to call it off, and Hamlet refuses in the quietest, strangest speech of the play. He will "defy augury," refuse to read the bad omen he clearly senses, because the timing of death is not ours to control: "If it be now, 'tis not to come; if it be not to come, it will be now; if it be not now, yet it will come. The readiness is all" (5.2). Set that beside "To be, or not to be." There, death was a dread that stopped thought cold. Here, it is simply the one certain appointment, and the only thing in a person's power is to meet it prepared. The question that drove four acts has quieted into acceptance.

ONE HAMLET, TWO STANCES TOWARD DEATH
ACT III · "TO BE, OR NOT TO BE"
Death is an unknown to dread; "the dread of something after death" freezes action. Thinking about it prevents doing anything.
ACT V · "THE READINESS IS ALL"
Death is a certainty to accept; its timing is not ours. The task is not to solve it but to be ready. Acceptance frees him to act.
The change is the heart of Act V: the same mind, no longer paralyzed by the question it could not answer.

READING JOURNAL
Set "the readiness is all" (5.2) beside a line from "To be, or not to be" (3.1) in your Journal. In four or five sentences, name what has changed in how Hamlet faces death, and then take a position: is his new calm hard-won wisdom, or is it a kind of giving up? Quote both lines, cite them, and argue from the difference between them. There is no official answer; defend yours from the text.
🤖 WORKING WITH AI

An AI tool can summarize how Hamlet escaped the ship and gloss the word "augury," and it will usually get those facts right. Whether his Act V calm is wisdom or resignation is the interpretive question of the whole act, and the play does not answer it; if an AI hands you a verdict, treat it as one reading to argue with, and go back to the two soliloquies yourself.
