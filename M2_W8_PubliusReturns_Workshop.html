💡 WORKED EXAMPLE (study how this reads — yours will argue a different question)

This example argues a question that is not on any Feeder menu: should a citizen have a legal duty to report a neighbor's minor wrongdoing, even when no one is harmed, for the good of the community? Studying how this one is built will not hand you your own op-ed.

Reflect: The question is genuinely contested: neighbors already disagree about how much they owe a community versus how much privacy of conscience they are owed in return. My own instinct is to distrust a legal duty to inform; I want to test that instinct against Publius, Emerson, and Douglass before I commit to it in print.

Brainstorm: My claim: the law should encourage citizens to report wrongdoing, but it should not compel it by force, because compelled conscience is not the same thing as civic virtue. The strongest objection I must answer: a community cannot function if every citizen treats reporting as optional, and Madison's whole argument for structure assumes citizens will sometimes need to be bound, not merely persuaded.

Organize (claim / concession / counter / evidence): Claim — the law may ask, but should not force, a citizen to report. Concession — Madison's structural logic has real force here: "ambition must be made to counteract ambition" (Federalist 51), and a republic cannot run on good intentions alone. Counter — but Emerson's warning that "nothing is at last sacred but the integrity of your own mind" ("Self-Reliance") shows why compelling a conscience by law can corrupt the very thing it claims to protect. Evidence — Douglass's Narrative supplies the historical warning: laws that compelled ordinary citizens to report people fleeing bondage turned neighbors into informants against the innocent, and his own account of finding "the pathway from slavery to freedom" (ch. 6) through a law meant to control him shows how a compelled duty can be defied by the very conscience it tries to bind.

Stylize (opening in Publius's register):
"Fellow citizens, a law that commands you to inform upon your neighbor asks for more than your testimony; it asks your conscience to become an instrument of the state. History has already shown us what such a law can produce, and a wise republic will ask a citizen's virtue to volunteer what it will not permit itself to seize by force."
🧠 PART A · REFLECT

Start with the civic question you are choosing to argue, from a Feeder menu or one of your own.

What is the contested civic question you will argue? State it as a single question a reasonable person could answer either way.
Where do you currently stand, before you have written a word of the op-ed? Be honest about your instinct, not your polished position.
Why does this question matter to real citizens right now? Name who is actually affected by the answer.
💬 PART B · BRAINSTORM

An op-ed needs a claim a reader could quote against you, and an objection strong enough to be worth answering.

State your claim in one sentence, plain enough that a stranger could repeat it back to you.
What is the strongest objection to your claim, stated fairly enough that someone who holds it would agree you have it right? This is not a strawman; it is the version of the other side that would actually worry you if you heard it.
🧩 PART C · ORGANIZE

Lay out a claim / concession / counter / evidence outline before you draft a single paragraph. Draw on at least two of the three module texts (the Federalist, "Self-Reliance," Douglass's Narrative) to fill it in.

Claim (your position, one sentence)
Concession (the opposing view, stated fairly, with a text behind it)
Counter (how you answer the concession, with a text behind it)
Evidence (your two module texts + the JStor source you will synthesize)
✍️ PART D · STYLIZE

Now put your outline into Publius's measured, formal register: the voice of a citizen addressing fellow citizens on a matter that concerns them all.

HALLMARKS OF PUBLIUS'S REGISTER
Addresses the reader directly as a fellow citizen, not an opponent ("fellow citizens," "you will agree").
Pairs an abstract principle with a concrete stake, so the reader feels why the principle matters.
Uses full, formal sentences: no contractions, no slang, no direct insults to those who disagree.
States the stakes plainly rather than hinting at them.
PRIVATE REGISTER
"I don't think people should have to snitch on their neighbors, that's kind of messed up."
PUBLIUS'S REGISTER
"Fellow citizens, a law that commands you to inform upon your neighbor asks for more than your testimony; it asks your conscience to become an instrument of the state."
Draft your opening (3 to 5 sentences): your hook and your claim, in Publius's register.
Apply the tool: take one sentence you drafted above, and if it slips into your private, everyday voice, rewrite it here in Publius's register.
Continue drafting your concession, counter, and evidence paragraph here (aim for the bulk of your 500 to 700 words).
Close (3 to 5 sentences): return to your hook and tell your fellow citizens plainly what you ask of them.
🤖 PART E · CHECK YOUR ARGUMENT WITH CLAUDE (THEN LOG IT)

Claude is a second pair of eyes on your reasoning, not a co-writer. Ask something narrow, such as: "What is the strongest objection to this thesis: [paste your claim]?" or "Does this evidence actually support this claim: [paste one paragraph]?" Read what Claude offers, decide for yourself whether it changes your concession, your counter, or your evidence, and revise in your own words if it should. Claude never drafts or words your op-ed for you, and if it names a source, you must verify that source yourself on JStor before you cite it; do not trust a title, author, or page number it gives you.
