📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
The founders did not decree the Constitution. They had to argue for it, in public, one newspaper essay at a time.
In 1787 the fate of the Constitution hung on persuasion. Before you read Madison, you need two things: the situation he was writing into, and the tools every persuader uses. Today you learn the founding moment and the three classic rhetorical appeals, ethos, logos, and pathos, so that when you read the Federalist you can name exactly how it works on a reader.

I will be able to explain why the founders argued for the Constitution in public essays.
2

3
I will be able to spot an appeal at work in a short piece of persuasion.

Why argue in the newspapers?

When the Constitutional Convention finished its draft in 1787, the document still had to be ratified, approved, by the states, and many Americans were suspicious of a stronger national government. So supporters took their case to the public. Writing under the shared pen name Publius, Alexander Hamilton, James Madison, and John Jay published essays in New York newspapers arguing, point by point, that the Constitution deserved support. These became The Federalist. The key thing to notice: this was not a command from above. It was persuasion aimed at ordinary readers who could vote the Constitution down. To read the Federalist well, you read it as an argument built to win a real audience.

🎪 PART 2 · THE THREE APPEALS

Since Aristotle, readers have named three ways a text persuades. Most strong arguments use all three; naming them lets you see how a writer is working on you.

THE RHETORICAL APPEALS
Ethos
credibility & character
Persuasion through the speaker's authority and trustworthiness. "Trust me: I am fair, informed, and on your side."
Logos
logic & evidence
Persuasion through reasoning: facts, causes and effects, if-then chains. The Federalist's main engine.
Pathos
emotion
Persuasion through feeling: fear of collapse, hope for the republic, pride, urgency.

🤖 WORKING WITH AI

An AI tool can define the three appeals and give textbook examples. Where it is weaker is judging a real, mixed passage, where a single sentence may work by logic and feeling at once. Use it to check a definition, then do the labeling yourself; the disagreements are where you actually learn to read rhetoric.
