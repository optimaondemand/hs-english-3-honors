📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
On his way into exile, Hamlet watches twenty thousand men march to fight over a field not worth farming, and asks why he, with every reason to act, still has not.
Hamlet has killed the wrong man, and Act IV opens with the bill coming due. The king moves fast: Hamlet is packed onto a ship for England before the body is cold. But on the road to the coast, Shakespeare stages one last meeting. Hamlet crosses paths with the army of young Fortinbras, the Norwegian prince from Act I, and out of that encounter comes his final soliloquy. Today you read scenes 1 through 4 and learn the device the encounter is built on: the foil.

The fallout, scene by scene

In scene 1, Gertrude reports the killing to Claudius: her son, "Mad as the sea and wind" (4.1), heard a stir behind the curtain, cried "A rat, a rat" (4.1), and ran the old man through. Watch the king's first reaction. It is not grief for Polonius; it is "It had been so with us, had we been there" (4.1). He hears the sword-thrust as meant for himself, and he immediately worries how the killing will look: "It will be laid to us" (4.1). By the end of the scene he has his answer: ship Hamlet away at dawn. Scene 2 is short and strange: Hamlet has hidden the body and answers the courtiers sent to find it in riddles, treating Rosencrantz as the king's sponge, soaked with favors and squeezed dry when needed. In scene 3, brought before the king, Hamlet keeps up the grim clowning. Asked where Polonius is, he answers that the old man is at supper, "Not where he eats, but where he is eaten" (4.3), and follows the thought all the way down: "a king may go a progress through the guts of a beggar" (4.3). It sounds like madness, but notice what the joke actually says to a usurping king: crowns do not protect a body. Claudius announces the voyage, "For England" (4.3), and Hamlet goes almost cheerfully. Then, alone, the king drops the mask in a soliloquy of his own. The letters Hamlet carries order England to kill him: "Do it, England, / For like the hectic in my blood he rages" (4.3). A fever, he says, and Hamlet's death the cure.

⚔️ PART 2 · THE DEVICE OF THE SCENE: THE FOIL

A foil is a character set beside another so that the contrast sharpens both. The word comes from an old jeweler's trick: a thin sheet of metal foil placed behind a gem to make it shine harder. In scene 4, Shakespeare places Fortinbras behind Hamlet. The two princes match point for point: each is the son of a dead king, each is named for his father, each has an uncle on the throne that might have been his. Then the match breaks. Fortinbras is marching an army across Denmark to attack Poland, and the prize, a passing captain admits, is "a little patch of ground / That hath in it no profit but the name" (4.4). He would not pay five ducats to farm it. Fortinbras acts on the thinnest pretext imaginable, an eggshell of a cause; Hamlet, with a murdered father, a corrupted court, and a direct charge from the dead, has not acted at all. That is what a foil does: it does not tell you who is right, it makes the difference impossible to ignore.

ACT IV, SCENE 4: TWO PRINCES, MEASURED AGAINST EACH OTHER
⚔️ FORTINBRAS, THE FOIL
His cause: a worthless field. "A little patch of ground / That hath in it no profit but the name" (4.4).
His response: twenty thousand men on the march, honor at stake in a straw.
💭 HAMLET, THE GEM
His cause: a murdered father, a stained mother, a throne held by the killer. By his own count he has "cause, and will, and strength, and means" (4.4).
His response: so far, one killing he did not intend and a ship carrying him away from his revenge.
The foil is the plainer metal that makes the gem's flaw visible. Hamlet looks at Fortinbras and sees exactly what he himself has not done.
💭 PART 3 · THE LAST SOLILOQUY: FROM QUESTION TO RESOLUTION

Left alone on the plain, Hamlet begins: "How all occasions do inform against me / And spur my dull revenge" (4.4). Everything he sees has become evidence at his own trial. You have now heard him alone many times, and this speech completes a pattern. The Act I soliloquy was an outburst of grief; "To be, or not to be" was a meditation with no I in it; this one is a self-examination that ends in a verdict. Follow its argument in three moves. First, the question: what is a man whose only business is "to sleep and feed? A beast, no more" (4.4). Reason was given to be used, not "to fust in us unused" (4.4). Second, the evidence against himself: he cannot even name what has stopped him, and the fact stands that "I have cause, and will, and strength, and means / To do 't" (4.4). Third, the example in front of him. He reasons that true greatness is "not to stir without great argument" (4.4), yet greatness also means finding honor's quarrel in a straw when honor is truly at stake, and his stake could not be greater. The speech ends on the play's hardest turn: "My thoughts be bloody or be nothing worth!" (4.4).

Read that last line carefully before you take it at face value. Hamlet resolves that his thoughts will be bloody, not his hands, and he makes the resolution while under guard, sailing away from the man he has sworn to kill. Whether this is a real turning point or one more substitution of words for deeds is a genuinely open question. Mark the line and hold your verdict; the rest of the play will argue it with you.

🔍 A CLOSER LOOK: WHAT ACTUALLY MOVES HIM

Look again at what convinces Hamlet, not just what he concludes. He does not find a better argument for revenge; he watches Fortinbras march twenty thousand men to die over "a little patch of ground / That hath in it no profit but the name" (4.4), and the sight shames him into his resolution. He is admiring, and measuring himself against, a war he himself calls pointless. That complicates a speech that is often read as simple heroic resolve. Is envying a needless slaughter a mark of honor working itself out in him, or a sign that Hamlet, like the foil he is watching, has started to mistake the size of the gesture for the rightness of the cause?

READING JOURNAL
In your Journal, answer this in four or five sentences: what exactly does the sight of Fortinbras's army change in Hamlet, and what does it leave unchanged? To do it well: quote one line from the soliloquy that shows the change (the opening line and the closing line are both strong candidates) and cite it (4.4); then point to one fact of Hamlet's situation at the end of the scene that is exactly what it was at the start. The gap between those two is the point.
🎤 RECITATION REHEARSAL: BEGIN MEMORIZING (ungraded, nothing to turn in)

By now your soliloquy is chosen and you have read it aloud across several weeks. This week, start committing it to memory. Work in small blocks: take the first two or three lines, say them until they come without looking, then add the next block and run from the top. Let the verse help you; the five-beat pulse and the punctuation are a built-in memory aid. Aim to have the first half solid from memory by the end of this week. The graded recitation is in Week 7, so this start gives you two weeks of margin.

🤖 WORKING WITH AI

An AI tool can outline the argument of "How all occasions do inform against me" and label Fortinbras a foil, because both are standard readings. What it cannot decide is whether the closing resolution is real, since the play itself leaves that open and Hamlet does not soliloquize again to tell us. It may also misquote: this soliloquy appears in some early texts of the play and not others, so quotations pulled from memory are unreliable. Check every line against the text you were assigned before you cite it.
