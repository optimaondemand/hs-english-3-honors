📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A father hires a spy to watch his own son. Then his daughter arrives, shaking, with a report of something we never see.
Act I ended with Hamlet swearing to remember and deciding to act mad. Act II opens somewhere stranger: not with Hamlet at all, but with Polonius coaching a servant in the art of spying on Laertes in Paris. Today you read Act II, scene 1, and study two tools the play now leans on: the surveillance motif, the pattern of watching and being watched that spreads through every scene, and reported action, the choice to keep an important event offstage and let us hear it only secondhand.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to trace the surveillance motif: who watches whom in scene 1, and by what method.
2
I will be able to explain what reported (offstage) action is and what a playwright gains by using it.
3
I will be able to weigh Ophelia's report of Hamlet's visit as filtered evidence, not settled fact.

A court that runs on watching

A motif is an image, action, or idea that keeps returning until the repetition itself starts to mean something. In Hamlet, the returning action is watching. Act I opened with sentries on watch for a ghost. Act II turns the watching inward: fathers watch children, kings watch nephews, and hired eyes do the work at a safe distance. Notice that almost no one in this act simply asks a direct question. They set traps, send agents, and, in Polonius's own words, "By indirections find directions out" (2.1). As you read, keep a running tally: who is watching whom, and who never realizes they are being watched?

👀 PART 2 · HOW A WATCHER/WATCHED PAIR WORKS

A watcher/watched pair has three parts: someone who wants information, an agent who does the actual watching, and a target who has no idea they are being observed. Scene 1 opens with the clearest example in the play so far. Polonius calls in his own servant, Reynaldo, and sends him to Paris, not to visit Laertes, but to test him: drop small false rumors about his son's behavior, "such wanton, wild, and usual slips" (2.1), and see which ones strangers confirm. As Polonius puts it, "Your bait of falsehood take this carp of truth" (2.1). Study how the three parts line up below.

Now read scene 1

Read Act II, scene 1. In the first half, watch how carefully Polonius scripts the spying: Reynaldo may hint at gambling and drinking, but must protect the family name from real "scandal." In the second half, someone arrives at Polonius's door badly shaken, with news about Hamlet. As you read that account, ask yourself who is really doing the watching in the exchange that follows, and whether the person delivering the report understands she is being used as one.

🎭 PART 3 · THE SCENE WE NEVER SEE: REPORTED ACTION

Hamlet's visit to Ophelia is one of the most vivid moments in the play, and Shakespeare deliberately keeps it offstage. We get it only as reported action: an event we never witness, delivered secondhand by a character who did. The choice does real work. Because we see nothing ourselves, we cannot rule on the central question. Is this the "antic disposition" Hamlet promised to put on, performed for a witness he knows will report it? Or is it genuine distress leaking through? A staged version would have to pick one. The report keeps both alive, and it makes us do exactly what everyone at Elsinore does: judge a person through someone else's account.

📓
READING JOURNAL
In your Journal, write three or four sentences on Ophelia's report. To do it well: first list what we actually know happened (only what her words describe), then name what is filtered, that is, anything colored by her fear or by having no one else to confirm it. Quote at least one physical detail from her speech, such as "Pale as his shirt" (2.1), and end with your own one-sentence verdict: performance, real distress, or impossible to tell from this evidence. Any of the three can be argued well; what matters is that your verdict rests on the words.
🤖 WORKING WITH AI

An AI tool can summarize scene 1 and even list the spying episodes for you. What it cannot do is weigh Ophelia as a witness, because that judgment depends on how you read her fear and her obedience to her father. AI summaries also tend to state Polonius's love theory as if the play endorses it; the text leaves it open. Read the scene yourself before you let any summary tell you what it says.
