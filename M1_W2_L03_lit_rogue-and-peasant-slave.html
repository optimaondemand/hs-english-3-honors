📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
An actor weeps real tears for an imaginary queen, and a prince who has real cause to weep asks himself why he has done nothing.
Act II ends with the arrival of a troupe of traveling actors and the play's second great soliloquy. Today you finish the act and study two techniques that lock together: the soliloquy of self-reproach, in which Hamlet turns his wit into a whip and uses it on himself, and the play-within-a-play, the device he invents in the same speech to test both the Ghost and the king.

I will be able to explain why the First Player's speech about Hecuba strikes Hamlet so hard.

📚 PART 1 · THE PLAYERS AND THE SPEECH ABOUT HECUBA

When "the tragedians of the city" (2.2) arrive, Hamlet comes alive for the first time since Act I. He greets them warmly and asks the First Player for an old favorite, a speech he prizes even though the play it came from failed; it was, he says, "caviary to the general" (2.2), too fine for the crowd. The speech retells the fall of Troy: Pyrrhus, a Greek soldier whose father was killed in the war, butchers the old king Priam while the queen, Hecuba, "the moblèd queen" (2.2), runs barefoot through the burning city. Listen to what the material is doing: a son avenging a father with a sword that falls without hesitation, exactly the act Hamlet has sworn and not performed. The actor is so caught in the telling that Polonius interrupts: "Look whe'er he has not turned his color and has tears in 's eyes" (2.2). A hired performer weeps over a fiction. Hamlet clears the room, and what that weeping unlocks in him is the soliloquy.

🔗 Read: Hamlet, Act II, scene 2, from the players' arrival to the end of the act (the soliloquy that begins "O, what a rogue and peasant slave am I!"). Folger Shakespeare, Hamlet 2.2.
🔥 PART 2 · THE SOLILOQUY OF SELF-REPROACH
A READING TOOL: THE SHAPE OF A SELF-REPROACH SOLILOQUY
1. THE ATTACK
The speaker turns hard against himself, naming his own failure in the harshest terms he can find.
2. THE REASONING
The attack cools into weighing: examining motives, testing evidence, questioning what he actually knows for certain.
3. THE RESOLVE
The speech ends not in another feeling but in a decision: a specific next step.
Not every self-reproach speech uses all three stages in equal measure, but watch for this shape: attack, then reasoning, then resolve. It is the map for reading the soliloquy below.

You know from Week 1 that a soliloquy shows the private mind. This one shows a mind attacking itself. Alone, back in verse, Hamlet measures himself against the actor and loses: "O, what a rogue and peasant slave am I!" (2.2). The actor summoned real tears "in a fiction, in a dream of passion," while Hamlet, with a murdered father and a real charge, stays "unpregnant of my cause" (2.2), that is, carrying his task without bringing it to birth. The self-contempt boils into a fantasy of what the actor would do with Hamlet's motive, then curdles into name-calling aimed at himself, and then, watch closely, the speech pivots. Insulting yourself is still doing nothing, and Hamlet catches himself doing it. Out of the collapse comes reasoning: the Ghost might be lying, since "the devil hath power / T' assume a pleasing shape" (2.2), so the charge needs independent proof before it deserves blood. And the actors themselves suggest the instrument. This is the signature shape of a Hamlet soliloquy: feeling first, then thinking about the feeling, then a decision the feeling alone could never have produced.

The central question of the speech is worth holding all module: "What's Hecuba to him, or he to Hecuba, / That he should weep for her?" (2.2). Why should pretended grief move a man more than real grief moves him? Keep your answer provisional; the play is not done with the question.

🎭 PART 3 · THE DEVICE: A PLAY WITHIN THE PLAY

Hamlet's plan is to have the players perform a murder resembling his father's before the whole court, and to watch Claudius watch it. Guilty spectators, he reasons, betray themselves; the stage has been known to make "guilty creatures sitting at a play" proclaim "their malefactions" (2.2). The device is called the play-within-a-play: a staged performance inside the world of the drama, watched by the characters while we watch them. It turns the act's whole surveillance pattern inside out. For two scenes, everyone has been spying on Hamlet; now Hamlet builds a trap where the theater itself does the spying, and the watcher becomes the watched. The act closes on his couplet: "The play's the thing / Wherein I'll catch the conscience of the King" (2.2).

A STAGE INSIDE A STAGE: WHO IS WATCHING WHOM?
OUTER STAGE · Shakespeare's Hamlet
We watch everyone. Nothing on either inner level escapes us.
MIDDLE STAGE · The court at Elsinore
Claudius watches the performance. Hamlet watches Claudius. Two audiences, two targets.
INNER STAGE · The players' murder play
A staged poisoning, built to resemble the crime the Ghost described. The bait at the center of the trap.
Each frame watches the one inside it. The fiction at the center is the only level where everyone finally sees the truth performed.

READING JOURNAL
Find the turn in the "rogue and peasant slave" soliloquy: the moment where Hamlet stops lashing himself and starts building the plan. Copy the line where you locate the pivot into your Journal, cited (2.2). Then write two sentences: what the speech is doing just before your line, and what it is doing just after. Readers place the turn in slightly different spots, so do not hunt for one official answer; what matters is that the before and the after genuinely differ in your quotation's shadow.
🎤 RECITATION THREAD · STARTS NOW (ungraded this week)

In Week 7 you will perform a graded recitation of a Hamlet soliloquy: memorized, spoken aloud, and recorded. The work starts now, because memory is built by small repeated visits, not a last-minute cram. This week, choose your soliloquy and write your choice in your Reading Journal. You may pick any soliloquy from the play, including ones we have not reached yet. Candidates you have already met or will meet soon:

• "O, that this too too solid flesh would melt" (1.2), grief in private
• "O, what a rogue and peasant slave am I!" (2.2), this week's self-reproach (you may recite a continuous portion; it is long)
• "To be, or not to be" (3.1), coming next week, the most famous and the most heavily judged

Pick the one whose voice you can imagine in your own mouth. You may change your mind through Week 4; after that, commit. Once you choose, read your soliloquy aloud two or three times this week. That is the whole assignment for now.

🤖 WORKING WITH AI

An AI tool can explain who Hecuba and Pyrrhus were and summarize the Troy story behind the First Player's speech; that background is factual and easy to check. It cannot locate the turn in the soliloquy for you, because that is a judgment about where feeling becomes reasoning, and different readers defend different lines. If you use AI for the mythology, still trace the speech's movement with your own pencil.
