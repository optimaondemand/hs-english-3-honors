WEEK 6 MAP
The Pathway from Slavery to Freedom
Last week you met the boy: the stolen childhood, the separations, the machinery of bondage. This week the Narrative turns on a single household in Baltimore, where an ordinary act, teaching a child his letters, becomes a crisis the moment someone tries to stop it. Watch what a determined mind does with that crisis across chapters 6 and 7: what a forbidden skill reveals about the people who forbid it, and what getting an education with no teacher and a law against the whole project actually costs the person who wants it. Here is everything for Week 6, in order.
🎯 THE QUESTION BEHIND MODULE 2
How do free people order their common life, how does a free person order an inner one, and what happens when a nation denies that self-government to some of its people?
📋 DO THIS, IN THIS ORDER
1
Lesson 2.21: Forbidden Knowledge (literature 1 of 3)
Chapter 6: a lesson begins, then a lesson is forbidden. Watch what the ban itself ends up teaching.

Lesson 2.22: Stealing an Education (literature 2 of 3)
Chapter 7: how a determined boy gets an education with no teacher and a law against the whole project, and what the getting costs him.

Lesson 2.23: Words as Power (literature 3 of 3)
Read Douglass as a writer: metaphor, antithesis, and cadence in the prose, and literacy as the first act of self-government.

Lesson 2.24: Grammar & Vocabulary (grammar + vocabulary)
Subordination and the complex sentence, the grammar of cause and consequence, plus Word Study from this week's reading.

Discussion: The Most Dangerous Skill (graded, post + one reply)
Why do the enslavers in the Narrative treat a child's alphabet as a threat, and what does Douglass gain and lose by learning to read? Cite one moment.
🎤 RECITATION: START NOW FOR NEXT WEEK
The graded Douglass recitation arrives next week (Week 7). This week's job is to choose and begin rehearsing your passage: a stretch of Douglass's own reflective or argumentative prose from the Narrative, about 45 to 75 seconds when spoken aloud. Chapters 6 and 7 are full of candidates, and Lesson 2.23 shows you what to listen for. Read your shortlist aloud once a day; a passage you can hear is a passage you can deliver.
