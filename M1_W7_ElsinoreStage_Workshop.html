💡 WORKED EXAMPLE (study how this reads — yours will be a different character)

This example uses Reynaldo, the servant Polonius sends to spy on Laertes in Paris (Hamlet 2.1). He is not on the feeder's character list, so studying this will not hand you your own scene.

Reflect: The moment right after Polonius finishes his instructions in 2.1, alone, before Reynaldo leaves for France. It matters because a minor character has to carry out his master's anxious, devious plan and has a private reaction to it that the play never shows.

Brainstorm: Reynaldo wants to do the job well and keep Polonius's favor, but the task troubles his own sense of honesty. The scene turns when he tests Polonius's logic, "by indirections find directions out," against his own conscience.

Organize (beats): Opening — alone, the instructions still ringing in his ears, uneasy. Turn — he turns the phrase "indirections... directions" over, testing whether it excuses the lying he has been told to do. Close — he sets the unease aside, telling himself a servant's obedience is its own kind of honesty, and departs.

Stylize (opening lines):
"My lord has bid me lie to learn the truth,
and call the crooked road the wiser way.
What honest man employs a crooked tool
and names the crookedness itself good craft?
Yet I am servant, and a servant bends."
🧠 PART A · REFLECT

Start with what you already know from your Week 4 Character Dossier.

Which secondary character will you write? (Ophelia, Laertes, Gertrude, Horatio, or Fortinbras)
What moment does the play leave off-page for this character? Name the exact seam: after which scene, during which offstage event.
Why does this moment matter? What would we understand about the character or the play that we do not get from the text alone?
💬 PART B · BRAINSTORM

A scene needs a character who wants something and a moment where that want shifts.

What does your character want in this scene? (Pull from the driving want you named in your dossier.)
What is the beat the scene turns on: the new thought, memory, or realization that shifts something?
🧩 PART C · ORGANIZE

Lay out a simple beat outline before you write a single line of verse. This becomes the spine of your draft.

Opening (the feeling or question)
Turn (the shift)
Close (the landing)
✍️ PART D · STYLIZE

Now put your outline into loose blank verse: unrhymed lines aiming for five stressed beats (da-DUM five times), the same line Shakespeare writes Hamlet's soliloquies in.

HEARING THE FIVE-BEAT LINE
da  DUM  ·  da  DUM  ·  da  DUM  ·  da  DUM  ·  da  DUM
My lord has bid me lie to learn the truth,
Five soft beats, five hard beats (gold). Read a line aloud and tap on each capitalized beat; if you tap more or fewer than five, the line may be running long or short.
Draft your opening 3 to 5 lines. Read each one aloud as you go.
Apply the tool: pick one line above, write it out again, and mark the five beats the way the visual does (capitalize or underline the stressed syllable).
Continue the rest of your scene here (aim for 20 to 40 lines total). Work in at least one antithesis and underline it once you have typed the final version in Word.
Director's note (3 to 5 sentences): what your character wants here, and why this scene fits the gap without contradicting the play.
🤖 PART E · CHECK YOUR RHYTHM WITH CLAUDE (THEN LOG IT)

Claude is a second pair of ears, not a co-writer. Paste your draft lines and ask something narrow: "Which of these lines breaks the five-beat rhythm? Just flag them, don't rewrite anything." Read what it flags, decide for yourself whether each flagged line should change, and fix it in your own words if it should. The lines, the word choices, and the decisions stay yours; Claude never drafts or rewrites your scene.
