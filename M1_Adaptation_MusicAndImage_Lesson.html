Music and Image: Telling a Story Without Words
Some of the most powerful moments in film use almost no dialogue. The score and the picture do the storytelling. As a director, this is one of your sharpest tools, in film and in VR.
🎵 DIEGETIC AND NON-DIEGETIC, ONE MORE TIME

Sound that belongs to the world of the scene (a car engine, a character humming) is diegetic. A musical score the characters cannot hear, laid over the scene to shape how you feel, is non-diegetic. Watch how each clip below uses non-diegetic music to carry meaning the images alone would not, and how the pace of the editing works with the music.

🎥 CLIP 1 · A WHOLE LIFE, NO DIALOGUE · Up (2009), "Married Life"

This is a wordless montage showing a marriage from wedding to widowhood, set to one recurring melody.

NOTICE (NO ANSWERS YET)
  • How does the melody change (key, tempo, instruments) as the story moves through different years?
  • What happens to the editing pace during the happy moments versus the sad ones?
  • What single repeated image keeps returning, and what happens to your feeling about it by the end?
Jot what you notice here, then copy it into your director's log or notebook.
🎥 CLIP 2 · DREAD BEFORE ANYTHING HAPPENS · The Shining (1980), opening

This clip shows a car driving through mountain scenery, filmed from above. Nothing frightening happens in the images alone. You will watch it twice, once without the score and once with it, to isolate what the music is doing.

A TWO-PART LISTENING ACTIVITY
Step 1. Mute your device (we cannot mute the clip for you), then watch the scene with the sound off. Just watch the road, the car, and the mountains.
Step 2. Unmute your device and watch the same scene again, this time with the sound on.
Step 3. Reflect. In your own words, how does the music add to this moment, when the images by themselves show nothing frightening?
🎥 CLIP 3 · TENSION WITHOUT AN ANSWER · Inception (2010), ending

This clip shows a spinning top and a cut to black at the very end of the film, without answering the question the story has been asking.

NOTICE (NO ANSWERS YET)
  • What is the score doing in the moments right before the cut to black?
  • Where exactly does the cut happen, and what does the sound do at that exact instant?
  • What do you feel in the silence that follows the cut?
Jot what you notice here, then copy it into your director's log or notebook.
