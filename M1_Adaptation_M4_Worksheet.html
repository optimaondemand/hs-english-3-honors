💡 WORKED EXAMPLE (study how this reads)

Here is a short model treatment for a text nobody in this course is adapting, Dickens's A Christmas Carol, so it will not overlap with your own idea. Notice how each part is concrete and how sound gets its own plan, not an afterthought.

Logline: A miser revisits three Christmases in a single night and chooses, for the first time, to change.

Through-line: The viewer moves as an unseen witness just behind Scrooge's shoulder, from Christmas Past to Present to Future.

Scene list: (1) Marley's warning in a cold, empty countinghouse. (2) Christmas Past: a warm, crowded schoolroom and a lost engagement. (3) Christmas Present: an overflowing, loud family table. (4) Christmas Yet to Come: a bare grave in near-total silence.

Spatial concept & sound: The viewer never speaks and is never seen by the characters, so the space itself feels like memory. Christmas Present uses rich, layered diegetic sound (carols, dishes, overlapping voices) to argue that this is a life Scrooge could still choose. Christmas Yet to Come strips almost all sound away, replaced by a single low non-diegetic drone, so the silence itself argues for dread.

Genre & tonal approach: Shifts from gothic dread (Marley) to warm naturalism (Present) to a stripped, funereal hush (Future), using the tonal shift itself as the argument that Scrooge's choice still matters.

Source list: Author Last, First. "Article Title." Journal Name, vol. #, no. #, year, pp. ##–##.
