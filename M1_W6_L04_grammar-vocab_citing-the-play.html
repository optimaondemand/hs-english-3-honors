Lesson 6.04 · Grammar & Vocabulary
Citing the Play in MLA
Lesson · one combined grammar and vocabulary session
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
You have been citing Hamlet as (act.scene) all module. Here is the full, correct way to do it, and how to quote verse.
Your discussion this week, and your project and any essay, all lean on quoting the play. This lesson gives you the MLA rules for citing a verse play by act, scene, and line, and for quoting poetry so the line breaks survive. It is short and practical; you will use it immediately.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to write an MLA parenthetical citation for a verse play by act, scene, and line.
2
I will be able to quote two or three lines of verse and mark the line breaks correctly.
3
I will be able to use two words from Act V precisely in sentences of my own.
✏️ PART 1 · THE RULES

For a play written in verse, MLA cites by act, scene, and line, separated by periods, in parentheses, with no "p." and no commas: (3.1.64) means act 3, scene 1, line 64. The period at the end of your sentence goes after the closing parenthesis. When you quote two or three lines of verse and run them into your sentence, mark each line break with a slash, with a space on either side. For a quotation longer than three lines, set it off as an indented block instead.

ANATOMY OF A VERSE QUOTATION
Hamlet resolves to trust something larger than his own plans: "There's a divinity that shapes our ends / Rough-hew them how we will" (5.2.11-12).
THE SLASH
A space, a slash, a space, at each line break, so a reader sees where the verse turns.
THE CITATION
act.scene.line, a range joined by a hyphen, in parentheses, sentence period after it.

READING JOURNAL
Practice on a line you have already logged. Pick any two consecutive lines of verse from your Journal notes, quote them inside a sentence of your own with the slash marking the line break, and add a correct MLA parenthetical. Then write one more sentence quoting just a short phrase (not full lines) and citing it. Check: spaces around the slash, periods (not commas) between act, scene, and line, and the sentence period after the parenthesis.
📕 PART 2 · WORD STUDY (from Act V)
WordIn the playWhat it meansWord origin
equivocation
noun		 In the graveyard, Hamlet marvels that "we must speak by the card, or equivocation will undo us" (5.1). Using words with double or slippery meanings to mislead without outright lying. From Latin aequivocus ("of equal or same voice"), from aequus ("equal") and vocare ("to call"); literally calling two different things by one name.
augury
noun		 Refusing to heed the omen he feels, Hamlet says "we defy augury" (5.2). The practice of predicting the future from signs or omens. From Latin augurium, from augur, the Roman priest who read omens in birds' flight; the word's deeper root is disputed, but is often linked to avis ("bird").
📓
READING JOURNAL · WORD STUDY
Add both words to the Word Study section of your Journal. For each, write one new sentence of your own on a subject unrelated to Hamlet, keeping the meaning exact and making the sentence show the meaning. For equivocation, aim for the sense of dodging with slippery words rather than lying outright. Notice how equivocation's root sense of "equal voice" still echoes in a statement built to mean two things at once.

💪 CARRY THIS FORWARD

Accurate citation is not busywork; it is how you let a reader check you and how you show you are arguing from the text, not from memory. Make it a habit now and it will serve you in every course.
