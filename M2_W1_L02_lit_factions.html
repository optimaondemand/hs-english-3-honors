📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A brand-new nation asks a nervous question: now that we are free, what will tear us apart?
Writing as "Publius" in 1787, James Madison names the greatest danger he sees to the young republic: the faction, a group that puts its own interest above everyone else's. Today you read the first half of Federalist No. 10 and follow Madison's reasoning about what factions are, where they come from, and why he says you cannot cure them by taking away freedom.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to define a faction in Madison's terms and name its causes.
2
I will be able to explain his "air and fire" comparison between liberty and faction.
3
I will be able to trace the cause-and-effect structure of Madison's argument.
🏛️ WHEN AND WHY THIS WAS WRITTEN

The Revolution had been won, but the young United States was struggling under the Articles of Confederation, a weak national government that left the states quarreling. In 1786, Shays' Rebellion, an armed tax revolt by Massachusetts farmers, frightened leaders who feared the whole experiment might collapse. To win support for a new Constitution, Madison, Alexander Hamilton, and John Jay published a series of newspaper essays under the shared pen name Publius. Federalist No. 10 is Madison's answer to a hard question: can a government by the people survive its own people's divisions?

📚 PART 1 · WHAT IS A FACTION, AND WHERE DOES IT COME FROM?

Madison defines a faction as a group of citizens, "whether amounting to a majority or a minority of the whole," who are "united and actuated by some common impulse of passion, or of interest, adverse to the rights of other citizens, or to the permanent and aggregate interests of the community" (Federalist 10). In plainer terms: a faction is a group that pushes its own goal even when it harms everyone else. And note the danger Madison flags, a faction can be a majority. Where do factions come from? From human nature itself: as long as people are free to think, they will reach different opinions and pursue different interests. Madison points to one cause above all: "the most common and durable source of factions has been the various and unequal distribution of property" (Federalist 10). The rich and the poor, creditors and debtors, merchants and farmers, each will pull toward its own advantage.

🔥 PART 2 · MADISON'S IMAGE: LIBERTY AND FACTION

If factions are so dangerous, why not simply outlaw them? Because, Madison argues, you cannot kill faction without killing freedom itself. He makes the point with one of the most memorable images in American political writing.

"LIBERTY IS TO FACTION WHAT AIR IS TO FIRE" (FEDERALIST 10)
💨
Air
feeds fire
=
🔥
Liberty
feeds faction
Take away air and the fire dies, but you cannot remove all the air without destroying life itself. Likewise, you could end faction by ending liberty, but then you would have destroyed the very thing the republic exists to protect. So the cure cannot be less freedom. It must be a design that controls the effects of faction. That is the problem Madison sets up here and solves in the second half of the essay.
🔗 Read: The Federalist No. 10, first half (through Madison's statement that the cure must control faction's effects, not remove its causes). Public-domain text: The Avalon Project, Yale Law School.
