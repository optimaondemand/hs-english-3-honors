📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
You have read five acts one scene at a time. Today you step back and see the whole shape at once, and turn it into an argument.
A strong literary essay does not summarize a play; it argues a claim about it. Today you trace Hamlet's arc across the five acts, name what makes him a tragic hero, and draft a thesis: a single arguable sentence answering whether Hamlet is admirable, self-defeating, or something more complicated. This is the thinking your Module 4 essays and every literature course ahead will ask for.

📚 PART 1 · A WARM-UP BEFORE WE BEGIN
💭 QUICK CHECK-IN (no wrong answer)
Now that you have finished the play: in one gut reaction, is Hamlet more admirable or more self-defeating?

The whole arc, and the tragic hero

Seen whole, Hamlet is the story of a brilliant mind learning, too late and at great cost, how to act. A tragic hero is a figure of real stature whose downfall comes partly from outside forces and partly from within, from a flaw or fatal tension in the character. Hamlet's stature is his intelligence and moral seriousness; his tension is that the same thinking that makes him admirable also stalls him. The play never resolves whether his delay is wisdom (he refuses to kill on a ghost's word until he has proof) or weakness (he talks where others strike). That unresolved tension is not a defect in the play; it is the play. Your job as a reader is not to eliminate it but to take a position within it.

📈 PART 2 · FIVE ACTS AT A GLANCE

Track one thread, Hamlet's relationship to action, across the play. The evidence for any thesis about him lives in how these moments line up.

HAMLET AND ACTION, ACT BY ACT
ACT I
Charged
Grief, the Ghost, the sworn task. "The time is out of joint."
ACT II
Self-reproach
Shamed by an actor's tears; devises a test instead of striking.
ACT III
Proof, then pause
The Mousetrap works; he spares the king at prayer, kills Polonius by mistake.
ACT IV
Resolve in words
"My thoughts be bloody," said while shipped into exile.
ACT V
Ready, and acts
"The readiness is all"; kills Claudius at last, while dying.
The arc bends from a task refused to a task completed. Whether that bend is growth or tragedy is yours to argue.

⚖️ A HERO WORTH ARGUING ABOUT

Keep the picture balanced as you draft your thesis. The same man who reasons his way to sparing Claudius at prayer also stabs blindly through a curtain, on a guess, and kills an old man who was never his enemy. The same man who spends four acts weighing what he owes his father's ghost rewrites his old friends' death warrant and sends Rosencrantz and Guildenstern to be executed in his place, then tells Horatio afterward, "they are not near my conscience" (Act V, scene 2). Both moments are in the text; neither cancels the other out. A strong reading of Hamlet has to hold his intelligence and moral seriousness in the same hand as his capacity for sudden, deadly self-preservation.

💬 WHERE DO YOU LAND?
Given the whole arc, which comes closest to what the text actually shows?
📓
READING JOURNAL
Draft your thesis. Write one arguable sentence answering: is Hamlet admirable, self-defeating, or something more complicated? To make it arguable, it must be a claim someone could reasonably dispute, not a fact. Then, underneath, write one sentence stating the strongest version of the opposing view. You will need both when you write analytically later in the year. A test: if no one could disagree with your sentence, it is a summary, not a thesis; sharpen it until it takes a side.
🤖 WORKING WITH AI

An AI tool will happily generate a thesis about Hamlet, and that is exactly the wrong use of it here. The thesis is the one thing that must be yours, because it is your judgment the rest of the year builds on. If you want, use AI after you draft to play devil's advocate, ask it for the strongest objection to your claim, then answer that objection yourself, from the text.
