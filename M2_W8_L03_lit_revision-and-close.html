Lesson 2.31 · Project Craft
The Revision Workshop
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Madison revised. Emerson revised. Douglass revised. A public argument is not finished when it is drafted; it is finished when it can survive its readers.
You have a full draft of your op-ed and, likely, notes from your teacher. Today you revise with purpose, before you submit the final later this week. Revision is not proofreading; it is re-seeing. You will test the argument's shape first, then sharpen the sentences that carry it, and only then proofread and verify every source. An op-ed signs a name to a claim in public. Before Publius signs, Publius checks.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to revise an argument in three passes: shape, sentence, proof.
2
I will be able to test my concession for fairness and my counter for strength.
3
I will be able to verify every quotation and source and cite the module's primary documents in MLA form.
🔄 PART 1 · THREE PASSES, IN ORDER
💭 QUICK CHECK-IN (no wrong answer)
Your draft came back with notes on it. Before you change a word, what does a marked-up draft mean?

Do not revise everything at once. Make three separate passes, big to small, so you do not polish a sentence you later cut.

REVISE IN THREE PASSES
Pass 1 · Argument & shape
Is the claim still clear from headline to close? Does the concession state the opposing view fairly, and does the counter answer that view, not a weaker one? Do at least two module texts and your JStor source appear where the argument actually needs them? Cut anything that wanders. This pass matters most.
Pass 2 · The sentence
Read aloud. Sharpen the concession-and-counter turns; make a balanced sentence actually balance; check that ethos, logos, and pathos are in proportion for a public readership; keep the Publius voice steady with no slip into your private one.
Pass 3 · Proofread & verify
Only now: spelling, punctuation (no em dashes), word count 500 to 700. Then verify: every quotation checked against the text, every source opened by you on JStor, every in-text citation matched to a works-cited entry in the MLA shapes below.
⚖️ PART 2 · THE FAIRNESS TEST

The concession is where op-eds most often cheat, and readers can tell. In Pass 1, hold your concession-and-counter paragraph to three questions. Could a thoughtful person who disagrees with you read your concession and say, yes, that is my view? Does your counter answer that view, or a flimsier one you invented for the occasion? And after the counter, does the concession keep its dignity, or did you dismiss it as nonsense? Meeting a real objection honestly is what separates argument from noise. Here is the difference on the page:

BEFORE · THE CHEAP COUNTER
"Some will say this proposal goes too far, but they are wrong."
No real view is conceded, so nothing real is countered. The reader who disagrees stops reading here.
AFTER · CONCEDE, THEN COUNTER
"Some will say this proposal goes too far, and they are right to worry: power granted for good reasons rarely stays small. But the founders' answer to that worry was not to give up on law. It was to make power check power, and this proposal does exactly that."
The worry is stated so well the opposition would sign it; then the counter answers that worry on its own terms.
🧩 NOW YOU: WHICH REVISION PASSES THE FAIRNESS TEST?
Same three questions, a different civic question: should social media companies moderate political speech? Which sentence actually grants the opposing view fairly and then answers it?
🔖 PART 3 · MLA FOR THE MODULE'S DOCUMENTS

Editions of the primary documents vary, so cite them in text by structural landmark, the way this module has all quarter: the Federalist by paper number (Federalist No. 51), Emerson by paragraph ("Self-Reliance" par. 12), Douglass by chapter (Narrative, ch. 7), and your JStor article by author and page (Author 214). In the works-cited list, use these shapes, replacing the details with those of the edition and article you actually used:

WORKS CITED · MODEL ENTRIES
Madison, James. "The Federalist No. 51." 1788. Project Gutenberg, address of the edition you used.
Emerson, Ralph Waldo. "Self-Reliance." Essays: First Series, 1841. Project Gutenberg, address of the edition you used.
Douglass, Frederick. Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass, an American Slave. 1845. Project Gutenberg, address of the edition you used.
Author Last Name, First Name. "Title of the Article." Name of the Journal, vol. number, no. number, year, pages. JSTOR, stable address from the article page.

Alphabetize by the first word of each entry and keep the hanging indent. The stable address for a JStor article is on the article's own page; use it, not the address bar of your search.

📓
READING JOURNAL
Before you revise, log your plan: list each note your teacher left (or, if none yet, the two weakest spots you can see yourself), and next to each write what you will do about it and why. After Pass 2, copy in one sentence of yours, before and after, and name what changed. Keeping this short record is the habit that turns a draft into a finished piece, and it is your proof of revision.
🤖 WORKING WITH AI

An AI tool can proofread spelling and mechanics, a fair use in Pass 3. It cannot verify your sources: AI tools invent citations, complete with plausible authors and page numbers, so every entry in your works cited must be a source you opened on JStor yourself. And do not let it rewrite your argument or smooth your concession; a generic paragraph in the middle of your op-ed reads like a stranger interrupting Publius mid-sentence.

➡️ NOW FINISH IT
📰
Publius Returns: Final
Submit your revised op-ed as the graded project, due at the end of this week. Optionally, record a 2 to 3 minute excerpt as a civic address.
💪 CARRY THIS FORWARD

The discipline you used here, revise big to small, concede fairly, verify before you sign, is the discipline of every honest public writer. Madison argued to a nation under a borrowed name; what made the name trustworthy was the checking behind it.
