Lesson 7.04 · Grammar & Vocabulary
Sentence Variety & Word Study Review
Lesson · one combined grammar and vocabulary session
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Prose that is all one length is prose a reader stops hearing. Rhythm is a tool, and you already have the parts.
Across this module you learned subordination, appositives, semicolons and colons, and parallelism. Today you put them to work on the thing they exist for: sentence variety, the mix of short and long, simple and complex, that makes writing move. A short review, then a last pass through the module's vocabulary before Module 2.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to vary sentence length and structure on purpose, for effect.
2
I will be able to use the module's vocabulary accurately in my own writing.
🎵 PART 1 · RHYTHM, ON PURPOSE

A run of same-length sentences flattens even good ideas. The fix is not one perfect sentence but the interplay of several. Use a short sentence to land a point after a long one. Use subordination to fold a minor idea under a major one. Save the semicolon for two thoughts that belong together. Read the two versions below aloud and hear the difference.

SAME PARTS, DIFFERENT RHYTHM
ALL ONE LENGTH (flat)
Hamlet gets proof of the king's guilt. He does not act. He spares the king at prayer. He kills Polonius by accident. He is sent away.
VARIED (moving)
Hamlet finally has his proof; still, he does not act. He spares the king at prayer, reasoning himself out of the kill, and minutes later stabs the wrong man behind a curtain. Then he is gone, shipped toward England before the body is cold.
Same facts, same grammar tools you already learned. The second one uses a semicolon, subordination, and one short clause for contrast, so a reader hears the stall and the lurch.
✍️ PART 2 · SPOT THE EFFECTIVE REVISION

Flat original: "Ophelia grows confused. She sings strange songs. She drowns in the brook." Three students revised it for variety. Which revision actually uses the tools from this module on purpose, rather than just adding words or stringing clauses together with "and"?

💬 WHICH REVISION SHOWS REAL SENTENCE VARIETY?
Choose the revision that varies structure on purpose, not just length.
📓
READING JOURNAL
Find a flat, list-like passage in your own recent writing (a feeder, a discussion post, or your project plan). Rewrite it in two or three sentences of varied length and structure, using at least one tool from this module (subordination, an appositive, a semicolon or colon, or parallelism). Keep both versions in your Journal and note, in one line, what the revision made a reader hear that the original did not.
📕 PART 3 · WORD STUDY REVIEW

No new words this week. Instead, look back over the module's Word Study: countenance, prodigal, rank, unction, equivocation, augury, and the words from Weeks 2, 3, and 5. These belong to you now if you use them.

📓
READING JOURNAL · WORD STUDY
Pick the two words from this module you found hardest to keep straight. Write one fresh sentence for each, on any subject, that makes the meaning unmistakable from context alone. If you can do that, the word is yours. Flag any word you still cannot use confidently so you can watch for it in Module 2.
