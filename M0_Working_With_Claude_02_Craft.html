Prompting, Records, and Checking
Four habits that make AI useful and honest: ask well, keep records, use it lean, and verify everything.
✍️ HABIT 1 · WRITE A GOOD PROMPT

A vague request gets a vague, generic answer. A specific one gets something you can actually use. A strong prompt usually has four parts:

ROLE / CONTEXT
Who you are and what you are doing. "I am an 11th grader analyzing Act I of Hamlet."
THE TASK
Exactly what you want it to do. "Explain this line," not "help with Hamlet."
THE MATERIAL
Give it the actual text or your actual notes, so it works from your reading, not a guess.
THE LIMITS
What you do NOT want. "Do not rewrite it; just point to the problem." "Two sentences, plain words."

📝 HABIT 2 · KEEP A RECORD (THE AI-USE LOG)

Whenever you use Claude on graded work, keep a short, honest log. It protects you (it shows the work is yours) and it makes you notice how you are actually using the tool. Four columns are enough:

What I asked
What it gave me
What I kept
What was wrong
Check my ten lines for broken meter.
Flagged lines 4 and 7 as having six beats.
Fixed line 4 myself; kept line 7 on purpose.
It miscounted line 2; I checked and it was fine.

Keep this log in your Module Organizer, in a section named "AI-use log." Projects that invite Claude will ask for it.

⚡ HABIT 3 · USE IT LEAN (MANAGING TOKENS)

AI tools measure work in tokens, small chunks of text (roughly a few characters each). Every word you send and every word it sends back costs tokens, and your access has limits. You do not need to count them, but a few habits keep you from running out mid-project:

▸ Send only what is needed. Paste the paragraph you are working on, not the whole play.
▸ Start a fresh conversation for a new task. Long, wandering chats carry all their earlier text along and cost more each turn.
▸ Ask for the length you want. "Two sentences" costs far less than an unbounded answer, and is usually more useful anyway.
▸ Do the easy thinking yourself. Save the tool for the moment you are genuinely stuck.
🔍 HABIT 4 · VERIFY EVERYTHING

This is the most important habit. AI tools sometimes state false things with total confidence. The classic trap in an English class: you ask for a quotation and it hands you a line that is not in the play, worded just plausibly enough to fool you. It can also get a date, an author, or a source wrong.

⚠️ THE RULE

Never put an AI-supplied quotation, date, or fact into your work until you have confirmed it against the real source with your own eyes. A quote is only yours to use once you have found it in the text and can cite it by act, scene, and line.

💾 SAVE YOUR OUTPUTS TO ONEDRIVE

Anything you decide to keep, save into your Optima → ELA → ELA11 folder, named the usual way (LastName_FirstName_AssignmentShortName_ELA11, with _Draft1 at the very end for drafts). A chat window is not storage; if you close it, the conversation can be gone. Your OneDrive folder is where your real, submittable work lives.

💪 CARRY THIS FORWARD

Ask well, log honestly, use it lean, verify everything, and save what you keep. Do those five things and Claude will make you faster without making the work any less yours. Each project will tell you exactly where it fits; the thinking stays with you.
