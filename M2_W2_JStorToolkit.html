📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Publius argued from evidence: history, law, the record of failed republics. When you write as a citizen this quarter, you argue from evidence too. This is where you learn to find it.
Your Publius Returns op-ed and this week's Feeder 4 both require scholarly sources, and your library for them is JStor, a large digital archive of peer-reviewed journal articles and primary documents. Today you learn the whole workflow: get in, search well, judge what you find, make sources work together, and cite them in MLA. Bookmark this page; you will use it all year.

🔍 STEPS 1 AND 2 · SEARCH WELL, THEN FILTER

JStor rewards short, precise searches. Start broad, then narrow:

Use two or three key terms, not a full question. "checks balances congress" finds more than "does the system of checks and balances still restrain congress today." Pull your terms from this week's lessons: separation of powers, judicial review, judicial independence, federalism, majority tyranny.
Put an exact phrase in "quotation marks" to keep the words together: "judicial review" as a phrase, not two stray words.
Then filter: in the results sidebar, limit the content type to journal articles (that is where peer review lives) and set a date range if your question is about the present day.
Read the abstract, the short summary at the top, before committing to a full article. It tells you in one minute whether the source actually addresses your question.
You may ask Claude to suggest search terms for your question. Do not ask it to name articles: AI tools routinely invent titles, authors, and journals that do not exist. Only cite a source you found and opened on JStor yourself.
⚖️ STEP 3 · JUDGE THE SOURCE (THE C.R.A.P. CHECK)

Being on JStor is a good sign, but it is not a verdict. Ask four questions of every source before you lean on it:

Currency
When was it written? For founding-era history, older scholarship can hold up; for a live constitutional debate, recent work matters.
Relevance
Does it address your actual question, or just mention your topic on the way to another one? The abstract usually settles this.
Authority
Who wrote it, and where was it published? A scholar in a peer-reviewed journal carries more weight than an unsigned piece.
Purpose
Is the piece informing, arguing, or advocating? You may still use an argumentative source; just know its angle before you borrow its claims.
🧵 STEP 4 · SYNTHESIZE, DON'T JUST QUOTE

Research writing fails most often not from bad sources but from stacked ones: quote, quote, quote, the writer nowhere in sight. To synthesize is to make sources talk to each other in service of your claim. A strong synthesis paragraph moves like this: state your claim; bring in source one to support or complicate it; bring in source two and show how it agrees with, extends, or pushes against source one; then conclude with what you now hold, which should be sharper than what you started with. The word that separates synthesis from stacking is the connective tissue: "Where Smith sees X, Chen finds Y," "Chen's data sharpens Smith's claim," "Both concede Z." If your two sources never touch, you have a pile, not a paragraph.

This is exactly what Feeder 4 asks you to do this week, with two sources, once, well.

Optional step: once you have found and read both sources yourself on JStor, you may ask Claude to check whether your synthesis paragraph describes each source accurately, or to point out where two sources are only sitting side by side instead of talking to each other. Claude cannot find or verify a source for you; it can only be trusted to react to text you paste in. Log what you asked and what you verified in one line at the end of your document.

📝 STEP 5 · SAVE THE MLA CITATION AS YOU GO

On any JStor article page, look for the "Cite" option: it generates a formatted citation you can copy. Choose MLA, paste it into your working document immediately, and note the page number of anything you quote. Do this the moment you decide to use a source; re-finding a lost article at midnight before a deadline is a misery you can skip. Two forms to keep straight:

In-text citation, inside your paragraph: the author's last name and page in parentheses, like (Ellis 24), right after the quote or borrowed idea.
Works-cited entry, at the end of your document: the full citation JStor generated. Check it against an MLA guide; auto-citations are close but not always perfect, especially capitalization.
📓
READING JOURNAL
Do a practice run now, before Feeder 4. Pick one question this week raised for you (for example: does life tenure still protect judicial independence, or has the confirmation process politicized it?). Search JStor with two or three key terms, filter to journal articles, and record in your Journal: your search terms, the titles and authors of two promising articles, and one sentence per article from its abstract saying what it argues. Run the C.R.A.P. check on the stronger of the two and jot your four one-line answers. This log becomes your head start on Feeder 4.
