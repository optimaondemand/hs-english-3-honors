📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
When ideas do the same job in a sentence, give them the same shape. Readers feel the balance before they can name it.
In Week 1 you learned subordination, which ranks ideas. Today's tool is its partner: parallel structure, which balances them. Parallelism means putting items that share a job into the same grammatical form. When the forms match, a sentence reads as deliberate and clear; when they do not, readers stumble without knowing why. Then two words from Act III for your Word Study.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to spot faulty parallelism: items in a series that do not share a grammatical form.
2
I will be able to revise a sentence so its series, pairs, and comparisons run parallel.
3
I will be able to use two Act III words precisely in sentences of my own.
✏️ PART 1 · MATCHING FORM TO MATCHING JOB

The rule: items joined by and, or, or a comma series should wear the same grammatical clothes. If the first item is an -ing phrase, the rest should be -ing phrases; if the first is a full clause, the rest should be clauses. The same holds for paired constructions (not only… but also, either… or, both… and) and for comparisons with than or as: whatever form sits on one side must sit on the other. Watch a broken series get repaired.

FROM OFF BALANCE TO IN BALANCE
Hamlet spends the performance watching his uncle, trading jokes with the queen, and he narrates the plot.
Two -ing phrases, then a full clause. The third item does the same job in a different form, and the series buckles.
↓
Hamlet spends the performance watching his uncle, trading jokes with the queen, and narrating the plot.
Three matching -ing phrases. Same jobs, same shapes: the sentence now reads as one controlled motion instead of three loose parts.

Two more repairs to study. Paired construction, faulty: The Mousetrap is not only a test but also it threatens the king. Parallel: The Mousetrap is not only a test but also a threat. Comparison, faulty: Claudius fears what Hamlet knows more than losing his crown. Parallel: Claudius fears what Hamlet knows more than what he might lose. In each case the fix is the same: find the two things being joined or compared, then give them one shape.

🧩 SELF-CHECK: WHICH VERSION IS TRULY PARALLEL?
"Hamlet's plan was … the king, … his face, and … the trap." Which completed version keeps all three parts in the same grammatical form?

READING JOURNAL
Repair these two sentences in your Journal. (1) "Horatio agrees to watch the king, taking notes on his face, and that he will compare impressions afterward." (2) "Hamlet wants either to confirm the Ghost's story or proof that it lied." To do it: underline the items being joined, name the form of the first item (clause? -ing phrase? to + verb?), then rewrite the others to match it. Finish by writing one three-item parallel sentence of your own about anything from this week's scenes, and read it aloud; balanced items have an audible rhythm.
📕 PART 2 · WORD STUDY (from Act III)

Two words from this week's scenes, defined where they appear.

WordIn the playWhat it meansWord origin
orisons
noun (plural)		 Hamlet greets Ophelia at her (staged) prayers: "Nymph, in thy orisons / Be all my sins remembered" (3.1). Prayers; a formal or private act of praying. From Old French oraison, from Latin orare ("to speak, to pray"); the same root gives "oration" and "oracle," all rooted in speaking aloud.
temperance
noun		 Hamlet tells the players that even in a whirlwind of passion they must acquire "a temperance that may give it smoothness" (3.2). Self-restraint; moderation that keeps strong feeling under control. From Latin temperantia, from temperare ("to mix in due proportion, to moderate"); the same root gives "temper," all meaning balance rather than excess.
📓
READING JOURNAL · WORD STUDY
Add both words to the Word Study section of your Journal. For each, write one new sentence of your own on a subject that has nothing to do with Hamlet, keeping the meaning exact. For example, for temperance: "Her temperance in the argument, steady voice, measured words, won over people her shouting never would have." Aim to make the sentence show the meaning, not just contain the word. Notice the example is also parallel: "steady voice, measured words." Notice, too, how temperance's root sense of "mixing in due proportion" still echoes in the idea of keeping feeling balanced rather than excessive.
