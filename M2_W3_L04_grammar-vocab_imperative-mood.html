📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
"Trust thyself." Two words, no stated subject, and a whole philosophy. The shortest sentences in Emerson's essay are commands, and commands have a grammar.
Madison argued with conditionals; Emerson argues with the imperative mood, the sentence-form that stops describing the world and starts directing the reader. Today you learn how imperatives are built, how the softer hortatory command with let works, and when a command persuades rather than hectors. Then Word Study: two words this essay leans on, aversion and integrity.

✏️ PART 1 · THE MOOD THAT GIVES ORDERS

English verbs come in moods, and you have already met two this quarter. The indicative states what is: Society demands conformity. The subjunctive imagines what is not; it lived inside last week's contrary-to-fact conditional, in Madison's "If men were angels." The imperative does neither. It commands: Trust thyself. Its grammar has two signatures. First, the subject is the understood you: unstated, but present, which is why the command lands on whoever is reading. Second, the verb takes its base form, the bare verb with no ending: trust, insist, go. For a negative imperative, put do not or never in front of the base verb: never imitate. Punctuate a command with a period; save exclamation points for genuine emergencies, since a calm imperative carries more authority than a shouted one. There is also a third form, the hortatory command with let, which urges rather than orders and lets the writer include others, or even themselves, in the command: Emerson writes, "Let a man then know his worth, and keep things under his feet" ("Self-Reliance"). A direct command to the reader would have been know your worth; the let form turns the same demand into an invitation the whole room can accept together.

THE FRAME (EMERSON'S OWN SENTENCE)
[you] Insist on yourself; [semicolon] never imitate. ("Self-Reliance")
[you] = the understood subject, never written but always aimed at the reader. Gold = a direct imperative: base verb insist, first word of the sentence. Blue = a negative imperative: never + base verb imitate. The semicolon yokes the two commands into one balanced order, the positive and the negative of a single demand. Emerson's shortest form drops even the preposition: Trust thyself, base verb plus an old form of yourself. Two words, and the subject is you.

Why does persuasion love the imperative? Because it changes the reader's role. An indicative sentence lets you watch an idea from your seat; an imperative pulls you onto the stage: now you must obey, refuse, or argue back, and any of the three means you are engaged. But the mood is strong medicine. A page of commands stops persuading and starts hectoring, and the reader quietly leaves. Watch Emerson's rhythm: each imperative rides on indicative sentences that give reasons before or after it. That is the working rule for your own writing: earn each command with the reasoning around it, and spend imperatives the way you spend exclamation points, rarely and on purpose.

📕 PART 2 · WORD STUDY (from "Self-Reliance")
WordIn the textWhat it meansWord origin
aversion
noun		 Naming what society prizes and what it shuns, Emerson writes: "The virtue in most request is conformity. Self-reliance is its aversion" ("Self-Reliance"). A strong, settled dislike; also the thing that is disliked or turned away from. From Latin roots meaning "a turning away." In Emerson's sentence, self-reliance is the thing society turns away from. From Latin aversionem, from avertere ("to turn away"), from ab- ("away") and vertere ("to turn"); literally a turning away from something disliked.
integrity
noun		 Emerson sets it above every other authority: "Nothing is at last sacred but the integrity of your own mind" ("Self-Reliance"). Wholeness; the state of being complete and undivided, from Latin integer, "whole." Applied to character, it means being one person through and through, not honest merely by rule but undivided between what you believe, say, and do. From Latin integritas, from integer ("whole, untouched"), from in- ("not") and tangere ("to touch"); literally "untouched-ness," wholeness applied to character.

READING JOURNAL · WORD STUDY
Add both words to your Journal. For each, write one new sentence of your own on a subject unrelated to Emerson, keeping the meaning exact and letting the sentence show it. For aversion, make clear who turns away from what; "I have an aversion" alone shows nothing. For integrity, let the wholeness sense do the work: show a person undivided between belief, word, and act, not just someone following rules. Bonus move: cast one of your two sentences as an imperative or a hortatory let sentence from Part 1. Notice how integrity's root sense of "untouched wholeness" still echoes in what it means to call a person's character intact.

💪 CARRY THIS FORWARD

The imperative is the mood of writers who want something from the reader beyond agreement: they want action. Emerson commands the conscience, and in two weeks you will hear Douglass command a nation's. When your own writing finally needs a command, you will know how to build one and what it costs to use.
