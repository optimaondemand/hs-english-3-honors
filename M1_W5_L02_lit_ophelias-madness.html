📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A young woman whose father was killed by the man she loved walks into the throne room singing. No one can make her stop, and no one can look away.
Since Act I you have watched Hamlet perform madness on purpose, the "antic disposition" he announced before he ever played it. Scene 5 shows you the real thing, and the difference is the point. Ophelia has lost her father to a killing she cannot understand and been abandoned by the man she was told to love and then told to refuse. What is left of her comes out in fragments of old songs. Today you read the scene twice over: once for Ophelia, and once for her brother Laertes, who crashes into the same grief at full speed and reaches for a sword.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to contrast Ophelia's real madness with Hamlet's feigned "antic disposition," with lines from the text.
2
I will be able to explain how Shakespeare uses song and broken form to carry grief that plain speech cannot.
3
I will be able to describe how Laertes answers his father's death, and set his speed beside Hamlet's delay.
📚 PART 1 · SONGS, FLOWERS, AND A MOB AT THE DOOR

The scene opens with the court already afraid of her. A gentleman reports that "She speaks much of her father" (4.5), and Horatio urges that she be seen before she can "strew / Dangerous conjectures in ill-breeding minds" (4.5). Notice the court's instinct: even a grieving girl is first weighed as a political risk. Then she enters, singing scraps of old ballads about a dead lover and a lost girl: "He is dead and gone, lady, / He is dead and gone" (4.5). The songs braid her two griefs, a dead father and a lost love, until they cannot be told apart, and between verses she drops sentences that land harder than the singing: "I cannot choose but weep to think they would lay him i' th' cold ground" (4.5). Claudius, watching, names the avalanche he is standing under: "When sorrows come, they come not single spies, / But in battalions" (4.5).

Then the doors break open. Laertes is back from France with a crowd behind him crying him king, and his first demand is three words long: "Give me my father!" (4.5). Set his response to a murdered father beside everything you have watched Hamlet do since Act I. Laertes does not test the evidence, consult his conscience, or worry about damnation; he throws allegiance and salvation on the table in one breath: "I dare damnation. To this point I stand, / That both the worlds I give to negligence" (4.5). It is everything Hamlet reasoned himself out of, performed in a single entrance. And watch Claudius. Facing an armed rebellion led by a grieving son, the king does not flinch; he begins, smoothly, to redirect the weapon. Where that redirection goes is the next lesson.

Ophelia returns once more, and gives out flowers: "There's rosemary, that's for remembrance," and "there is pansies, that's for thoughts" (4.5); then fennel, columbines, rue, a daisy. For rosemary and pansies she names the meanings herself. For the rest, the text stays silent about who receives which flower and what each one accuses. Editors and actors have argued about it for four hundred years, which is exactly the effect: the maddened girl hands the court its portraits, and the audience is left to match them.

🔗 Read: Hamlet, Act IV, scene 5. Folger Shakespeare, Hamlet 4.5.
🎭 PART 2 · TWO MADNESSES: THE MASK AND THE BREAK

Shakespeare has been saving this comparison since Act I. Hamlet chose his madness; he announced in advance that he would "put an antic disposition on," and he takes the mask off whenever it suits him, speaking sanely to Horatio in one breath and wildly to Polonius in the next. His mad talk always has an edge and an audience; the riddles about worms and kings you read in scene 3 are aimed. Ophelia's madness chose her. It has no off switch, no target, and no strategy; it does not perform for the court, it simply spills. The play sets the two side by side so you can see what the real thing costs, and what it says about Hamlet that he could counterfeit it.

FEIGNED AND REAL: THE SAME COURT SEES BOTH
🎭 HAMLET: THE MASK
Chosen: announced in advance as an "antic disposition" (1.5).
Switchable: off with Horatio, on with the king and Polonius.
Aimed: the wildness carries barbs; even the worm-riddles warn a king (4.3).
🌷 OPHELIA: THE BREAK
Unchosen: grief for a father killed by the man she loved.
Unswitchable: no sane aside, no exit from it, in any company.
Unaimed: songs and flowers that expose the court without meaning to (4.5).
One madness is a tool; the other is a wound. The scene asks you to notice which one the court finds more frightening.

Notice the form on the page as well. Ophelia grieves in song: rhymed, metrical scraps of ballads everyone in the audience would have known. The play's reasoners work in blank verse; its broken heart sings. When a mind can no longer build an argument, it reaches for verses built long ago by someone else. That borrowed, secondhand quality is what makes the songs unbearable: the feeling is hers, but she can only say it in a dead form, the way mourners reach for old prayers. This is pathos, the moment a text stops arguing with you and simply makes you feel; and Shakespeare builds it here not with a great speech but with a nursery-simple tune in the wrong room.

READING JOURNAL
Choose one fragment of Ophelia's singing or one sentence she speaks between songs, quote it exactly, and cite it (4.5). Then write three or four sentences: what real loss is inside the fragment, and why does it land harder as a song scrap than it would as a plain statement? To do it well, name the two griefs the scene keeps braiding together and say which one your fragment carries, or whether you honestly cannot tell; that undecidability is worth writing about too.
🤖 WORKING WITH AI

If you ask an AI tool what Ophelia's flowers mean, it will likely hand you a confident table: fennel for flattery, rue for regret, and so on, often with a definite recipient for each. Be careful. The play itself names meanings only for rosemary and pansies; the rest is a long tradition of editors' guesses, and the text assigns no recipients at all. An AI presents that tradition as fact because it cannot tell the difference between what the text says and what readers have said about it. You can. Cite the play for what the play says, and label everything else as interpretation.
