💡 WORKED EXAMPLE (study how this reads)

Here is a strong short analysis of the crop-duster clip. Notice that it names film-grammar terms and says what each choice argues. Yours will be about a different scene.

"In the crop-duster scene, Hitchcock uses shot scale and mise-en-scene to make Thornhill helpless. The wide shots shrink him against an empty landscape, so the setting itself argues that there is nowhere to run and no one to help. The near-total absence of non-diegetic sound makes the ordinary daytime feel wrong, and when the plane finally roars in, the sudden noise after silence spikes the tension. The choice to set danger in bright, open farmland instead of a dark alley breaks a thriller convention, which argues that Thornhill is exposed even where he should be safe."
