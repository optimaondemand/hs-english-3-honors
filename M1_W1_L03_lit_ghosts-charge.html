📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A ghost that has said nothing all through Act I is about to speak. What it asks of the one person listening may cost him everything.
Act I closes out today. First a father, Polonius, sends his own children into the world with advice, including the line this course is named around: "to thine own self be true." Watch what he does right afterward that seems to test that very line. Then the Ghost finally speaks. Today you finish Act I and study a figure the play uses to hold opposites in tension: antithesis.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to state what the Ghost reveals and exactly what it asks of Hamlet.
2
I will be able to identify and build an antithesis: a balanced pairing of opposites.
3
I will be able to explain why Hamlet decides to "put an antic disposition on."
📚 PART 1 · ADVICE, THEN THE TRUTH

Scene 3 is a family goodbye before scenes 4 and 5 turn back to dread. As you read scene 3, notice who gives advice to whom, and watch for a phrase this whole course takes its title from: "to thine own self be true." Notice who says it, in what mood, and what he does right afterward that seems to test his own advice. Then in scenes 4 and 5, watch the one thing that has stayed silent through all of Act I finally speak. Read closely for three things as you go: exactly what it reveals, exactly what it demands of Hamlet, and just as tellingly, what it explicitly tells him not to do. Notice, too, how Hamlet behaves once it is gone, and what he asks his friends to swear.

🔗 Read: Hamlet, Act I, scenes 3–5. Folger Shakespeare, Hamlet 1.3.

⚖️ PART 2 · ONE FIGURE TO USE: ANTITHESIS

Antithesis sets two opposite ideas in a balanced grammatical frame so the contrast rings. You already met one in Hamlet's very first line: "more than kin, and less than kind." The balance itself carries the meaning. More is weighed against less; kin against kind; the matching shape makes the opposition feel sharp and deliberate. A whole play about the gap between seeming and being naturally speaks this way.

ANTITHESIS: TWO OPPOSED HALVES IN ONE FRAME
left pan
more than kin
⚖️
right pan
less than kind
Same frame ("[more/less] than [X]"); the words that change are opposites. That is the whole trick.
📓
READING JOURNAL
Write one antithesis of your own in your Journal, on any subject you like. To build it: choose a fixed frame ("not X but Y," or "more X than Y"), then drop a genuine pair of opposites into it, and check that the grammar of each half matches. Under it, note in a sentence what the balance makes a reader feel. You will reuse this move in Feeder 1.
