📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Most people treat changing your mind as a weakness. Emerson calls the refusal to change it a hobgoblin.
Last week you met Emerson's first target: conformity, the pressure to think what everyone around you thinks. Today he names a second, sneakier enemy: your own past. Why do we keep defending opinions we no longer hold? Emerson's answer produced one of the most quoted (and most misquoted) sentences in American writing, and it will sharpen how you handle your own evolving claims all quarter.

📚 PART 1 · A WARM-UP BEFORE WE BEGIN

The second terror

Last week Emerson argued that conformity, living "after the world's opinion," scatters your force and blurs your character. But he sees a second force that "scares us from self-trust": consistency, which he describes as "a reverence for our past act or word, because the eyes of others have no other data for computing our orbit than our past acts, and we are loth to disappoint them" ("Self-Reliance"). Notice the reasoning inside that sentence. We stay consistent, Emerson says, not because our old opinion is still true but because other people are watching, and their picture of us is built from what we already said. Consistency, on this account, is just conformity wearing a mask: conformity to your own past self.

👻 PART 2 · THE HOBGOBLIN SENTENCE

Here is the famous sentence, with the word people forget in bold. Read it slowly; every word is doing work.

ONE SENTENCE, THREE MOVES ("SELF-RELIANCE")
"A foolish consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds, adored by little statesmen and philosophers and divines."
"foolish"
The qualifier everyone drops. Emerson does not attack all consistency, only the kind kept for its own sake after the reason for it has died.
"hobgoblin"
A goblin from folklore, a fear for children. The metaphor does the arguing: only a small mind is frightened by the charge "but you said the opposite last year."
"little statesmen…"
He names the professions that live by their record: politicians, philosophers, clergy. The people most watched are most tempted to freeze in place.

Then Emerson pushes further: "With consistency a great soul has simply nothing to do" ("Self-Reliance"). And he gives the positive command that follows from it: "Speak what you think now in hard words, and to-morrow speak what to-morrow thinks in hard words again, though it contradict everything you said to-day." The standard is not "never contradict yourself." The standard is say what you now believe, plainly, every time. If your thinking has grown, your words must be allowed to grow with it. A person who forbids that growth is not being principled; that person is guarding a reputation.

⭐ PART 3 · "TO BE GREAT IS TO BE MISUNDERSTOOD"

Emerson knows the objection and stages it himself. He imagines a voice interrupting: "Ah, so you shall be sure to be misunderstood." His reply is a roll call: "Is it so bad, then, to be misunderstood? Pythagoras was misunderstood, and Socrates, and Jesus, and Luther, and Copernicus, and Galileo, and Newton, and every pure and wise spirit that ever took flesh. To be great is to be misunderstood" ("Self-Reliance").

Look at who is on the list: thinkers whose ideas contradicted the settled opinion of their day and were attacked for it, and whose ideas later reshaped the world. The logic runs one direction only. Emerson claims that every great spirit was misunderstood; he does not claim that everyone who is misunderstood is great. Being confusing, or wrong, or simply stubborn earns no place on that list. The sentence is a consolation for those who speak honest new thought, not a license to be careless.

Put the two halves of today together and Emerson's position comes into focus. Your past positions are data about who you were, not commands to who you are. Truth-telling in the present tense will sometimes look like contradiction from the outside, and that appearance is a price worth paying. Whether that price is always worth paying, especially for the people a leader has made promises to, is a question worth holding onto. Keep it open; it returns with force in Lesson 2.15, when Emerson meets Madison.

🤖 WORKING WITH AI

The hobgoblin sentence is one of the most misquoted lines in American literature; AI tools trained on the misquotations sometimes repeat them, dropping the word "foolish" or reshuffling the ending. An AI summary can also flatten Emerson's careful one-way claim about being misunderstood into "geniuses are always doubted." Check any quotation against the actual text before you use it; that habit is worth more than any summary.
