Sounding Like the Play

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
One modern word in the wrong place can break the whole spell. Fidelity is in the diction as much as the plot.
Your scene should feel like it belongs in Hamlet, and that is mostly a matter of register: the level and flavor of the language. You do not need to fake Elizabethan English or stuff in old words. You need to avoid the modern ones that yank a reader out of the world, and to keep your scene consistent with what the real play establishes. Today's lesson shows you how to check both.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to spot and remove anachronisms in diction and reference.
2
I will be able to match a plausible register without forcing archaic words.
3
I will be able to check my scene against the real plot for consistency.
🎭 PART 1 · KEEP THE SPELL, OR BREAK IT

Register is a dial, not a costume. Aim for elevated, formal, timeless English: full words instead of slang, images drawn from a world of candles and swords and sea-voyages rather than screens and cars. The fastest way to break the spell is a modern idiom or object. The examples below are invented to show the two directions; the point is the kind of word, not these exact ones.

WHAT KEEPS THE SPELL, AND WHAT BREAKS IT
KEEPS IT (elevated, timeless)
• Full words: "I cannot," not "can't" when weight is wanted
• Images from the play's world: night watch, poison, the sea
• Direct address: "my lord," "good friend"
• A plain strong verb over a fancy one
BREAKS IT (modern, out of world)
• Slang and filler: "okay," "totally," "you guys"
• Modern objects: phones, clocks by the minute, guns
• Therapy or business talk: "process my feelings," "leverage"
• Forced fake-old words you would not use twice

Note the trap on the right's bottom line: overcorrecting into fake Elizabethan ("forsooth," "prithee" in every line) breaks the spell just as badly as slang. Aim for clear, dignified English you could say with a straight face.

🧩 WHICH LINE BELONGS IN THE PLAY'S WORLD?
One of these is a genuine line from Hamlet (Act I, sc. 2); the other two are invented to show what breaks the spell. Which one is real?
🔍 PART 2 · CONSISTENCY WITH THE REAL PLAY

Fidelity is also about facts. Your character can only know what they would know at that point in the play. Check your draft against the real timeline: does your scene contradict anything Shakespeare shows before or after the seam you chose? If it does, either move your scene to a different gap or adjust it. That discipline, keeping invention inside the truth of the text, is exactly the accuracy habit this course keeps returning to.

🧩 DOES THIS BREAK THE TIMELINE?
Say your scene is set for the watch on the battlements in Act I, before the Ghost has said a word to Hamlet. Which detail would break consistency with the real play?
📓
READING JOURNAL
Run two checks on your draft and log them in your Journal. First, the register check: find any word or image that could not belong in the play's world and note a replacement. Second, the consistency check: write one sentence confirming your character only knows what they would know at your chosen seam, and name the moment in the real play just before and just after your scene. Fix anything that fails either check before you submit.
🤖 WORKING WITH CLAUDE ON THIS PROJECT

This is a good, honest use of Claude: ask it to flag possible anachronisms or plot contradictions, never to fix them for you. The Elsinore Stage Workshop page carries this exact step and the place to log what it flagged and what you verified and changed yourself.
