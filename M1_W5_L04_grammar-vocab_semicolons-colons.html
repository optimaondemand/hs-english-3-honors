Sentence Craft: Semicolons and Colons
Lesson · one combined grammar and vocabulary session
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A period separates. A comma pauses. The semicolon and the colon do something neither can: they show the exact relationship between two ideas.
You already build complex sentences with subordination and balance them with parallel structure. Today's tools finish the kit. The semicolon joins two complete thoughts that belong together; the colon aims a sentence at what follows it. Writers who reach for a dash every time two ideas touch are guessing; these two marks let you say precisely how the ideas connect. Then two words from Act IV for your Word Study.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to join two closely related independent clauses with a semicolon, and know when not to.
2
I will be able to use a colon to introduce an explanation, an example, or a list after a complete clause.
3
I will be able to use two Act IV words precisely in sentences of my own.
✏️ PART 1 · THE SEMICOLON: A HINGE BETWEEN EQUALS

A semicolon joins two independent clauses, two complete thoughts that could each stand as a sentence, when they are so closely related that a period would cut the thread between them. The test is simple and strict: cover the semicolon and read each side alone. If either side cannot stand as its own sentence, the semicolon is wrong. Do not use it with a coordinating conjunction (and, but, so); the comma owns that job. Use it when the second clause answers, sharpens, or turns against the first, and you want the reader to feel the two thoughts touch. One more use worth knowing: when the items in a list contain commas of their own, semicolons keep the boundaries visible (Fortinbras, the soldier; Laertes, the avenger; Hamlet, the thinker).

TWO MARKS, TWO JOBS
Choppy:  Laertes wants revenge. Hamlet wants certainty.
↓
Laertes wants revenge; Hamlet wants certainty.
SEMICOLON: two complete thoughts, held face to face. The contrast does the talking.
Vague:  The king's plan has several layers. There is a rigged blade and poison and a cup.
↓
The king's plan has three layers: an unblunted blade, a poisoned edge, and a poisoned cup.
COLON: a complete thought, then the delivery of what it promised.
The semicolon balances; the colon announces. Both sides of a semicolon must stand alone. Only the left side of a colon must.
🎯 PART 2 · THE COLON: A PROMISE KEPT

A colon follows a complete independent clause and delivers what that clause promised: an explanation, an example, a list, or a quotation. Think of it as an arrow. Everything before the colon must be able to stand alone as a sentence; everything after it pays off the setup. That is why a colon can never follow a fragment like The three foils are: the setup is incomplete, so there is nothing to keep the promise. Write The play gives Hamlet three foils: Fortinbras, Laertes, and the Player. In your literary writing this year, the colon is also the cleanest way to introduce quoted evidence: make your claim in a full clause, then open the door. Claudius diagnoses the disease he plans to exploit: "that we would do / We should do when we would" (4.7).

📕 PART 3 · WORD STUDY (from Act IV)

Two words from Act IV, defined where they appear.

WordIn the playWhat it meansWord origin
conjecture
noun		 Horatio warns that mad Ophelia may "strew / Dangerous conjectures in ill-breeding minds" (4.5). A conclusion formed by guesswork; an opinion built on incomplete evidence. From Latin conjectura, from conicere ("to throw together"), from com- ("together") and iacere ("to throw"); literally scattered clues thrown together into a guess.
contagion
noun		 Laertes vows to coat his blade with poison: "I'll touch my point / With this contagion" (4.7). A poison or disease that spreads by contact; more broadly, a harmful influence that passes from one person to others. From Latin contagio, from contingere ("to touch closely"), from com- ("together") and tangere ("to touch"); literally a "touching together," how disease was thought to spread.
📓
READING JOURNAL · WORD STUDY
Add both words to the Word Study section of your Journal. For each, write one new sentence of your own on a subject that has nothing to do with Hamlet, keeping the meaning exact. For example, for conjecture: "Until the flight recorder was found, every theory about the crash was conjecture." Aim to make the sentence show the meaning, not just contain the word. These two complete your Module 1 Word Study list through Week 5, which the Reading Journal Check will look for. Notice how conjecture's root sense of "throwing together" clues still echoes in a guess pieced together from incomplete evidence.
