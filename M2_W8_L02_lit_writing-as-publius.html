📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
An op-ed is an argument built to be read by a stranger on a busy morning. Every part of the form exists to earn the next thirty seconds of that stranger's attention.
You have your question, your position, and your three voices. Today you learn the vessel that carries them: the opinion editorial, the modern descendant of exactly what Publius was writing when he sent his essays to the New York newspapers in 1787. The form is short, public, and built from moves you have already practiced this module: the three appeals from Week 1, the concession-and-counter move from Feeder 3 and the Week 1 grammar lesson, and the source synthesis from Feeder 4 and the Week 2 research toolkit. This lesson shows you where each move belongs.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to plan an op-ed through its five parts: hook, claim, concession and counter, evidence, and close.
2
I will be able to adopt a pseudonymous public voice that argues to fellow citizens rather than at opponents.
3

📜 PART 1 · WHY A PSEUDONYM

When Hamilton, Madison, and Jay argued for the Constitution in the New York press, they signed all eighty-five essays with one invented name. The mask did real work. It let three very different men speak as one steady voice. It kept the argument in front and the personalities behind: a reader could not dismiss Federalist 10 by dismissing Madison, because the reader did not know it was Madison. And it placed the writers inside the audience they addressed; Publius writes as one citizen among citizens, with everything at stake that his readers have at stake. They were not alone. The ratification debate was crowded with masked voices arguing back, under names like Brutus, Cato, and the Federal Farmer.

🏛️ HISTORICAL CONTEXT · THE NAME ITSELF
"Publius" honors Publius Valerius Publicola, a founder of the Roman Republic remembered as a friend of the people. Choosing the name was itself an argument: it announced that the writers stood for a republic, before a single essay was read. Choose your own pseudonym the same way. A name like "A Student of the Republic" or "Vigilans" tells the reader what you stand for; a joke name spends your credibility before your first sentence. One honest boundary for our version: real op-ed pages today require real names, and your teacher knows exactly who you are. The pseudonym here is a craft exercise in building a public voice, not a hiding place.
📰 PART 2 · THE ANATOMY OF A PERSUASIVE OP-ED

An op-ed runs 500 to 700 words and earns its reader paragraph by paragraph. The five parts below are the load-bearing walls. Notice where the module's moves slot in: the appeals you met in Week 1 are distributed across the whole structure, the concession-and-counter move from Feeder 3 gets its own room near the middle, and the researched synthesis from Feeder 4 arrives where the argument needs outside weight.

ANATOMY OF A PERSUASIVE OP-ED (500–700 WORDS)
1 · THE HOOK (pathos leads)
One or two paragraphs. A scene, a startling fact, or a sharpened question that makes a stranger stop scrolling. Publius opened Federalist 1 by telling New Yorkers the fate of self-government itself hung on their decision. Raise the stakes honestly; do not manufacture them.
↓
2 · THE CLAIM (your position, early and plain)
One sentence a reader could quote against you. An op-ed is not a mystery novel; state your answer to the civic question by the end of the second paragraph and let everything after defend it.
↓
3 · CONCESSION AND COUNTER (ethos is built here)
Grant the strongest opposing point fairly, in its own best words, then answer it. This is the Feeder 3 move at full size, and the Week 1 grammar of concession carries it: although the objection holds real force, it fails at a specific, nameable point. A reader trusts a writer who can lose a small point gracefully.
↓
4 · THE EVIDENCE (logos does the heavy lifting)
Your module texts and your JStor research, synthesized rather than stacked: the sources speak to each other and to your claim, the Feeder 4 move. Brief quotations, MLA in-text citations, your own reasoning binding them together. Two texts plus one scholarly source, minimum.
↓
5 · THE CLOSE (pathos returns, with a charge)
Return to your hook so the piece lands where it launched, then tell your fellow citizens plainly what you ask of them: a vote, a habit, a change of mind. Publius always ended by pointing at the decision in front of the reader. So should you.

Two warnings from the form's long history. First, the parts are walls, not wallpaper: a reader should feel the structure without seeing labels, so never write "In conclusion" or "My concession is." Second, the proportions matter. If the hook swallows a third of your words, or the evidence shrinks to one rushed paragraph, the op-ed tips over. Claim and close are short; concession and evidence carry the middle weight.

🔍 PART 3 · FOLDING IN THE RESEARCH, MLA AND ALL

Your op-ed carries two kinds of sources, and each is cited the way this module has always cited it. The primary texts take their structural landmarks: a paper number for Publius (Federalist 51), the essay title for Emerson ("Self-Reliance"), a chapter for Douglass (Narrative, ch. 7). Your scholarly source takes the standard MLA in-text form, the author's last name with a page number where one exists, and every source you cite appears in a short works-cited list after the op-ed. Newspaper op-eds skip works-cited lists; yours keeps one because this is also a research apprenticeship, and the revision lesson later this week will check it.

Use the scholarly source where your argument needs weight your own voice cannot supply: a historical fact, a study, a scholar's judgment about what Madison or Emerson or Douglass meant. Introduce it with a signal phrase that tells the reader who is talking and why they should care, let it say one thing well, and return to your own voice. If you have not yet found your source, go back through your Feeder 4 search trail before opening a new one; the JStor toolkit from Week 2 walks you through search terms, filters, and the credibility checks a source must pass. The synthesis standard from Feeder 4 still governs: the source must interact with your texts and your claim, not sit beside them as decoration.

📓
READING JOURNAL
Build your op-ed skeleton in your Module 2 Organizer before you draft a single full paragraph. Write five lines, one per part of the anatomy: the image or fact your hook opens with; your claim as one quotable sentence; the opposing point you will concede and the specific place it fails; the two texts and one JStor source your evidence section will synthesize, each with its citation ready; and the charge your close leaves with your fellow citizens. Above the skeleton, write your pseudonym and one sentence on why you chose it. Coaching: draft the claim line first and the hook line last, because until you know exactly what you are claiming, you cannot know which thirty seconds of attention to fight for.
🤖 WORKING WITH AI

AI tools can suggest search terms and even name articles that sound perfect for your question, and some of those articles do not exist. AI-generated citations regularly point to invented authors, invented journals, or real journals with invented page numbers. Verify every AI-suggested source by finding it on JStor yourself and reading it before you cite it. What AI cannot do at all is take your position for you; an op-ed written by a machine under a pseudonym is two masks and no face, and your teacher is reading for the citizen underneath.
