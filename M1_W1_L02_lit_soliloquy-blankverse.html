Left alone for the first time, Hamlet says what he could not say to the court. Listen to how the lines move.
In Lesson 1.01 you watched Hamlet guard his face in public. Now the court clears and he speaks his private mind: his first soliloquy. Today you close-read that speech, and you learn the verse Shakespeare wrote it in, blank verse, the five-beat line. You will write in this line yourself this week, so learn its sound now.

📚 PART 1 · WHAT HAMLET SAYS WHEN NO ONE LISTENS

Alone, Hamlet wishes his own flesh would "melt / Thaw and resolve itself into a dew," and he calls the whole world "weary, stale, flat, and unprofitable." The cause pours out: his father barely two months dead, his mother already remarried to his uncle. His grief curdles into disgust, and he lands on a bitter generalization, "frailty, thy name is woman," before catching himself. The court saw a sulky prince; alone, we see a mind close to despair, disgusted by a marriage he thinks came too fast to be decent. Then he hears footsteps and shuts the door on all of it: "But break, my heart, for I must hold my tongue." The public mask goes back on mid-thought. That snap from private truth to enforced silence is the soliloquy's whole shape.

🔗 Re-read: Hamlet's first soliloquy in Act I, scene 2 (it begins "O, that this too too solid flesh would melt"). Folger Shakespeare, Hamlet 1.2.
🎵 PART 2 · THE VERSE IT IS WRITTEN IN: BLANK VERSE

Most of Hamlet is written in blank verse: unrhymed lines of iambic pentameter. An iamb is a soft beat then a hard beat, da-DUM; pentameter means five in a row. So the standard line runs ten syllables and five beats of stress. It sits close to natural English speech, which is why it can sound both ordinary and grand. Read the last line of the soliloquy aloud and lean on the capitals.

SCANSION · HAMLET, ACT I, SCENE 2
da  DUM  ·  da  DUM  ·  da  DUM  ·  da  DUM  ·  da  DUM
But break, my heart, for I must hold my tongue.
Five soft beats, five hard beats (gold). Ten syllables. One line of iambic pentameter.

The pattern is a frame the poet plays against, not a cage. When a line runs perfectly, the mind is steady; when the beat stumbles or an extra syllable spills over the end, something in the speaker is off balance. Notice, for instance, that the play's most famous line, "To be, or not to be, that is the question," carries an extra unstressed syllable at the end ("-tion"). That soft, unresolved ending is called a feminine ending, and it leaves the line hanging, unfinished, exactly like the question it asks.

💭 NOW YOU: SCAN A NEW LINE
Say this line from the same soliloquy aloud, slowly: "How weary, stale, flat, and unprofitable" (Act I, sc. 2). Compare its rhythm to "But break, my heart, for I must hold my tongue" above. Which is true?
📓
READING JOURNAL
Copy any one line of Hamlet's soliloquy into your Journal and mark its beats: read it aloud, then put a mark over each syllable your voice leans on. Aim to find five. Then write one sentence: does the line hold the steady da-DUM, or does it stumble, and if it stumbles, what is happening to Hamlet at that moment? There is no single right scansion; trust your ear and show your marks.
🤖 WORKING WITH AI

AI can count syllables and tell you whether a line "scans," and it is usually right about the count. It is less reliable about which syllables a person actually stresses, and it will sometimes force a natural line into a pattern it does not have. Your ear is the real test: read the line aloud and mark the beats you hear.
