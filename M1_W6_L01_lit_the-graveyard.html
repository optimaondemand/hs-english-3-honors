📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
The most serious scene in the play opens with two laborers cracking jokes and tossing skulls out of a grave.
After four acts of watching and plotting, Act V begins in a graveyard, and it begins funny. Two gravediggers trade riddles while they work; then Hamlet picks up a skull and finds it belonged to a man he loved as a child. Today you read scene 1 and study how Shakespeare places comedy right next to death, an old idea called memento mori, the reminder that death spares no one, however great they become.

📚 PART 1 · A WARM-UP BEFORE WE BEGIN
💭 QUICK CHECK-IN (no wrong answer)
Why do people sometimes crack jokes at a funeral or in a hospital waiting room?

Comedy at the edge of the grave

Shakespeare opens the final act with two clowns, the theatrical term for comic laborers, digging a grave and speaking in prose, not verse. They argue law and logic, sing while they dig, and treat bones as the tools of their trade. This is comic relief: a break in tension that, placed well, makes the seriousness around it land harder. Notice the clowns speak prose while the nobles speak verse; Shakespeare uses the shift to mark the difference between the daily world and the tragic one. When Hamlet arrives and asks how long a body lasts in the earth, the gravedigger answers with grim cheer, that a tanner's corpse "will last you some eight year or nine year" (5.1). Death here is not terror; it is a schedule.

💀 PART 2 · YORICK, AND THE GREAT LEVELER

Then the gravedigger hands up a particular skull, and the scene changes key. It belonged to Yorick, the old court jester who carried Hamlet on his back as a boy. Hamlet holds it: "Alas, poor Yorick! I knew him, Horatio: a fellow of infinite jest" (5.1). The lips that kissed him are gone; the jokes that filled the hall are silent. From this one skull Hamlet reasons outward to a hard truth: death makes no exceptions. Even the greatest are not spared. Alexander the Great, he imagines, is now dust that might plug a beer barrel; "Imperious Caesar, dead and turned to clay, / Might stop a hole to keep the wind away" (5.1). This is memento mori: the reminder that every life, jester or conqueror, ends in the same earth. The whole play has asked how to live rightly; the graveyard answers that the clock is short for everyone.

MEMENTO MORI: THE ONE END THAT LEVELS EVERYONE
The jester
Yorick, "a fellow of infinite jest," who once carried the prince on his back.
The conqueror
Alexander, Caesar, the greatest names Hamlet can summon.
→
The same dust
Clay that "might stop a hole to keep the wind away" (5.1). Rank buys no exception.
💔 PART 3 · THE FUNERAL, AND A LEAP INTO THE GRAVE

The comedy stops cold. A funeral procession enters, and the cut rites tell Hamlet the death was a suspected suicide before he understands whose it is. Then he sees Laertes grieving and realizes: the grave is Ophelia's. Grief overrules his hiding. He steps forward and matches Laertes claim for claim, insisting on the depth of his own love: "I loved Ophelia. Forty thousand brothers / Could not with all their quantity of love / Make up my sum" (5.1). The two men grapple. Whether Hamlet's outburst is pure love, guilt, or rivalry with Laertes is left for you to weigh; what is certain is that the prince who spent four acts watching from the shadows has stepped into the open, at a graveside, over a body his own actions helped fill.

READING JOURNAL
Write three or four sentences on why Shakespeare opens his most death-haunted scene with jokes. To do it well: quote one gravedigger line and one line from Hamlet over the skull, each cited (5.1), and explain what the comedy does to the grief that follows. Does the humor make the funeral hit harder, soften it, or something else? Argue from the two lines you chose.

🤖 WORKING WITH AI

An AI tool can tell you who Yorick was and explain the memento mori tradition, both easily verified. It cannot judge, for you, whether Hamlet's graveside outburst is love or rivalry; that is an interpretive call the text keeps open. If an AI states one reading flatly, treat it as a claim to test against the lines, not a settled fact.
