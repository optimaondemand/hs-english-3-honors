📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A man walks into a room to think about whether life is worth living. He does not know the room is bugged.
Act III, scene 1 holds the most famous speech in English drama and one of its cruelest conversations. Claudius and Polonius hide themselves to watch Hamlet with Ophelia, and everything Hamlet says lands twice: once on the person in front of him, and once on the listeners he cannot see. Today you read the "To be, or not to be" soliloquy as a new kind of thinking, and you learn the device that makes the nunnery scene sting: dramatic irony.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain how "To be, or not to be" differs from Hamlet's earlier soliloquies: a meditation, not an outburst.
2
I will be able to define dramatic irony and show how the hidden watchers change the meaning of the nunnery scene.
3
I will be able to support a claim about a double-hearing line with exact words from the scene.
📚 PART 1 · A WARM-UP BEFORE WE BEGIN
💭 QUICK CHECK-IN (no wrong answer)

A different kind of soliloquy

You have heard Hamlet alone before. In Act I he raged: his flesh should melt, the world was "weary, stale, flat, and unprofitable." This speech is different. It opens on the most general question a mind can ask: "To be, or not to be, that is the question" (3.1). Listen to what is missing. There is no I in it. Hamlet never says my father, my uncle, or revenge. He speaks of we and us, of anyone who must decide "Whether 'tis nobler in the mind to suffer / The slings and arrows of outrageous fortune" or "to take arms against a sea of troubles" (3.1). This is the meditative soliloquy: not a burst of feeling but a mind reasoning through a problem step by step, in general terms, as if writing a philosophy essay out loud.

Follow the argument's hinge. Death looks restful, "To die, to sleep" (3.1), until the thought turns: "To sleep, perchance to dream" (3.1). What dreams may come is unknowable, and that unknown, "the dread of something after death" (3.1), stops the reasoning cold. The speech ends where the whole play keeps ending: "Thus conscience does make cowards of us all" (3.1). Thinking deeply and acting boldly pull against each other. That is Hamlet's condition in a single line.

👀 PART 2 · THE DEVICE OF THE SCENE: DRAMATIC IRONY

Dramatic irony is the gap that opens when the audience knows something a character on stage does not. Before Hamlet enters, Polonius arranges this very meeting: he plants Ophelia in Hamlet's path, then hides with Claudius behind the arras to listen, calling the two of them "lawful espials" (3.1), lawful spies. As you read, track who is hidden, who is performing, and who is watching without being seen, and listen for anything a hidden character says that no one on stage can hear.

HOW DRAMATIC IRONY WORKS: LAYERS OF KNOWLEDGE
HIDDEN WATCHERS
See and hear everything; stay unseen themselves.
→
ON STAGE
Speak and act, knowing only part of the truth.
→
YOU, THE AUDIENCE
Often hold the most complete picture of anyone in the room.
When these levels do not match, a single line can mean one thing to the person it is spoken to and something sharper to whoever else is listening. That mismatch is dramatic irony.

💔 PART 3 · THE NUNNERY SCENE: EVERY LINE LANDS TWICE

Hamlet's greeting is gentle: "The fair Ophelia. Nymph, in thy orisons / Be all my sins remembered" (3.1). Then Ophelia, on instruction, returns his gifts, and the gentleness curdles. He denies ever loving her, orders her again and again, "Get thee to a nunnery" (3.1), and widens the attack to all pretense: "God hath given you one face, and you make yourselves another" (3.1). Read that last line with the watchers in mind. A man with two faces is standing behind the curtain.

Watch for two hinge moments. Mid-scene, Hamlet suddenly asks Ophelia where her father is; many readers take this as the moment he guesses the trap, and his cruelty after it as a performance for the curtain. The text leaves it open, so hold your judgment and mark the evidence. And near the end he says something the hidden king cannot afford to miss: those that are married already, "all but one, shall live" (3.1). The scene closes on two verdicts. Ophelia grieves the man she remembers, "O, what a noble mind is here o'erthrown!" (3.1), and Claudius, unconvinced by the madness story, decides Hamlet is a danger: "Madness in great ones must not unwatched go" (3.1).

READING JOURNAL
Choose one line Hamlet speaks to Ophelia and write three or four sentences on how the hidden watchers change it. To do it well: quote the line exactly and cite it (3.1); say what it means as words to Ophelia; then say what it becomes as words overheard by Claudius or Polonius. "God hath given you one face, and you make yourselves another" and "all but one, shall live" both reward this treatment, but any line you can argue from the text is fair game.
🎤 RECITATION REHEARSAL CHECKPOINT (ungraded, nothing to turn in)

Last week you chose the soliloquy you will perform in Week 7. This week, read it aloud from start to finish at least twice. Stand up, speak at performance volume, and let the punctuation set your pauses. Do not try to memorize yet; the goal is to get the argument of the speech into your voice. If you chose "To be, or not to be," today's lesson is your rehearsal guide: it is a meditation, so play the thinking, not the despair.

🤖 WORKING WITH AI

An AI tool can paraphrase "To be, or not to be" line by line, and its paraphrase will usually be serviceable. What it cannot settle is the scene's open question, whether Hamlet knows he is being watched, because the text itself does not settle it. If you ask, an AI may state one answer as fact. Treat that as a claim, not a verdict, and test it against the lines the way you would test a classmate's argument.
