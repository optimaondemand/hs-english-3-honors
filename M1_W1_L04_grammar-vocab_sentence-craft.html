📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Short, choppy sentences report facts. Complex sentences show which fact depends on which.
You are advanced writers, so grammar here is quick and immediately useful. Today's tool is subordination: joining ideas so one clearly leans on another. It is the single fastest way to turn flat, list-like prose into sentences that think. Then two words from Act I for your Word Study.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to tell an independent clause from a subordinate clause.
2
I will be able to combine two short sentences into one complex sentence using subordination.
3
I will be able to use two Act I words precisely in sentences of my own.
✏️ PART 1 · INDEPENDENT AND SUBORDINATE CLAUSES

A clause is a group of words with a subject and a verb. An independent clause can stand alone as a sentence: Hamlet distrusts the king. A subordinate clause cannot; it opens with a subordinating word (because, although, when, since, while, if) that makes it lean on something else: because his father's death still troubles him. By itself that is a fragment. Attach it to an independent clause and you get a complex sentence that shows a relationship, cause, contrast, or time, instead of just stacking facts. Below, two flat sentences become one that reasons.

FROM CHOPPY TO COMPLEX
Hamlet acts strangely. He wants to hide his true purpose.
↓
Because he wants to hide his true purpose, Hamlet acts strangely.
The shaded part is the subordinate clause. Notice the comma after it when it comes first, and how the sentence now shows why, not just what.
✏️ CHECK YOURSELF: IS IT PUNCTUATED CORRECTLY?
Which of these three versions of the same sentence follows the comma rule from the example above?
📓
READING JOURNAL
In your Journal, take these two flat sentences and combine them into one complex sentence: "The Ghost tells Hamlet the truth. Hamlet is not sure he can act on it." To do it: pick a subordinating word that fits the relationship (although, even though, because), turn one sentence into a clause that starts with it, and join it to the other. If your clause comes first, put a comma after it. Write two different versions using two different subordinating words, and note which one you prefer and why.
📕 PART 2 · WORD STUDY (from Act I)

Two words from Act I, defined where they appear.

WordIn the playWhat it meansWord origin
countenance
noun		 Horatio describes the Ghost's face as one "more in sorrow than in anger." A person's face or expression; the look the face is wearing. From Old French contenance, from Latin continentia ("restraint, self-control"), from continere ("to hold together"); the sense narrowed from bearing to the face itself.
prodigal
adjective		 In scene 3, the young are warned against being too free, too prodigal, with what they give away. Spending or giving lavishly, often more than is wise. From Latin prodigere ("to squander"), from pro- ("forth") and agere ("to drive"); literally "to drive forth," which became spending wastefully.
📓
READING JOURNAL · WORD STUDY
Add both words to the Word Study section of your Journal. For each, write one new sentence of your own on a subject that has nothing to do with Hamlet, keeping the meaning exact. For example, for countenance: "The coach kept a calm countenance even as the game slipped away." Aim to make the sentence show the meaning, not just contain the word. Notice how countenance's root sense of "holding together" still echoes in the idea of keeping your composure.
