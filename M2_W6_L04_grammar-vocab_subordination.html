Lesson 2.24 · Grammar & Vocabulary
Subordination: The Sentence That Ranks Its Ideas
Lesson · one combined grammar and vocabulary session
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Douglass almost never reports an event and stops. Something happens, and because it happens, something follows. The sentence that carries that kind of reasoning is the complex sentence, and it has a grammar.
Emerson argued in commands; Douglass reasons in subordination, sentences where one clause takes charge and the others report to it. Today you learn to build complex sentences, to choose which idea rules the sentence and which serves it, and to punctuate the result. Then Word Study: two words from chapter 7, stratagem and abolition, one of which Douglass had to define for himself.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to tell an independent clause from a subordinate clause and build complex sentences with subordinating conjunctions.
2
I will be able to use subordination to rank ideas, signaling cause, time, and concession, and punctuate each arrangement correctly.
3
I will be able to use two words from this week's reading precisely in my own sentences.
✏️ PART 1 · CLAUSES THAT RULE, CLAUSES THAT SERVE

A clause is a group of words with its own subject and verb. An independent clause can stand alone as a sentence: I found time to get a lesson. A subordinate clause has a subject and verb but cannot stand alone, because a subordinating conjunction in front of it marks it as serving some larger sentence: when I was sent of errands. Join one of each and you have a complex sentence. The conjunction you choose declares the relationship: because, since, and as signal cause; when, after, while, and whilst (Douglass's older form) signal time; although, though, and whereas signal concession, the move you practiced in Week 1; if and unless signal condition, Madison's mood from Week 2. Two working rules govern the punctuation: when the subordinate clause comes first, close it with a comma before the main clause arrives; when the main clause comes first, you usually need no comma. And never let a subordinate clause stand alone with a period after it; the conjunction promises a main clause, and a reader feels the broken promise. That stranded clause is called a fragment.

THE FRAME (DOUGLASS'S OWN SENTENCE, CH. 6)
Whilst I was saddened by the thought of losing the aid of my kind mistress, [comma] I was gladdened by the invaluable instruction which, by the merest accident, I had gained from my master. (Narrative, ch. 6)
Blue = subordinate clauses. The first opens with whilst and cannot stand alone; it holds the sorrow. The second, opening with which, is a relative clause serving the noun instruction. Gold = the independent clause, the one part of the sentence that could survive by itself: I was gladdened. Look at what the ranking accomplishes. Both feelings are real, but the grammar decides the case: the sorrow is filed in a subordinate clause, the gladness governs the sentence. The comma after the opening clause marks the handoff. Douglass renders a fair verdict on a mixed experience in a single sentence, and subordination is the instrument that does it.

You have now collected three ways to join ideas, and they make a set. Coordination, the balanced sentence from Week 4, sets two clauses side by side as equals; Douglass's "What he most dreaded, that I most desired" gives each half the same weight on purpose. Subordination makes one clause serve another, which is exactly what tracing cause and consequence requires: because Auld forbade the lessons, Douglass understood their value. The main clause carries your claim; the subordinate clause carries its circumstances. When you revise your own paragraphs this week, ask of every compound sentence: are these two ideas really equals, or is one of them evidence for the other? If one is evidence, subordinate it, and your reasoning will surface in the grammar itself.

READING JOURNAL
Write three complex sentences of your own about this week's reading, each tracing a cause or consequence from chapters 6 and 7. Build one with a cause conjunction (because or since), one with a concession conjunction (although or while), and one with a time conjunction (when or after). Bracket the subordinate clause in each, label the job it does (cause, concession, time), and check the comma rule: comma after a subordinate clause that opens the sentence, usually none before one that ends it. 📕 PART 2 · WORD STUDY (from the Narrative, chapter 7)
WordIn the textWhat it meansWord origin
stratagem
noun		 Of learning to read and write with no teacher and a watching household, Douglass writes: "In accomplishing this, I was compelled to resort to various stratagems" (Narrative, ch. 7). A careful scheme or maneuver for gaining an end against watchful opposition. From a Greek word for a general's maneuver in war; the word still carries that scent of a contest, of outwitting rather than overpowering. A stratagem is planned, not improvised, which is what separates it from a mere trick. From Greek strategema, from strategos ("general"), from stratos ("army") and agein ("to lead"); literally "the act of a general," a planned maneuver in war.
abolition
noun		 Douglass keeps hearing the word before he can learn it: "If a slave ran away and succeeded in getting clear… it was spoken of as the fruit of abolition" (Narrative, ch. 7). The dictionary gave him only "the act of abolishing," and no adult would tell him what was to be abolished. The formal, deliberate ending of a practice or institution, in this era above all the ending of slavery; from a Latin verb meaning to destroy or efface. An abolitionist is one who works for that ending. Note the word's precision: things merely stop, but only decisions abolish. From Latin abolere ("to destroy, blot out"), likely ab- ("away") joined to a root meaning "to grow"; literally an undoing of something's continued existence.

Look closely at the second row: in chapter 7 Douglass performs a word study of his own. The dictionary fails him, the adults refuse him, so he collects the word's contexts, everything a slaveholder calls "the fruit of abolition," until a city newspaper finally lets him triangulate the meaning. That is inference from context, done under conditions no vocabulary quiz will ever impose on you, and it is the same discipline this column asks of you every week.

READING JOURNAL · WORD STUDY
Add both words to your Journal. For each, write one new sentence of your own on a subject unrelated to Douglass, keeping the meaning exact and letting the sentence show it. For stratagem, make the contest visible: show who is maneuvering around whom, and toward what end; a stratagem with no opposition is just a plan. For abolition, let the formality do the work: show something ended by decision and effort, not something that faded on its own. Bonus move: build one of your two sentences as a complex sentence from Part 1, with the subordinate clause bracketed and labeled. Notice how stratagem's root sense of "a general's maneuver" still echoes in its scent of outwitting an opponent.

Discussion: The Most Dangerous Skill
Your post this week traces causes and consequences: why enslavers feared literacy, and what learning to read caused in Douglass. Those are subordination's home questions. Let a because clause carry your reasoning and an although clause carry the loss you are weighing, and keep your main clauses for the claims themselves.
💪 CARRY THIS FORWARD

An op-ed that only asserts is a list; an op-ed that subordinates is an argument. When Publius Returns asks you to persuade the citizenry, your reader will decide within a paragraph whether you rank your ideas or merely pile them. Douglass, who reasoned his way from a forbidden alphabet to a nation's conscience one consequence at a time, is the standard. Next week you recite him.
