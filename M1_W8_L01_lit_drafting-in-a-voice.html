Lesson 8.01 · Project Craft
Drafting in a Character's Voice
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A soliloquy is a shape, not just a mood. Give your character a beginning, a turn, and an end, and the lines will follow.
You have a plan: a character, a gap in the play, and something they want. Today you turn that plan into a draft. The trick is structure. Every soliloquy you studied this quarter moved through a shape, from a starting feeling or question, through a turn, to a decision or new understanding. Build your scene on that shape and the blank verse becomes a container to fill, not a blank page to fear.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to shape my scene into an opening, a turn, and a close.
2
I will be able to write lines that mostly hold the five-beat rhythm and land one antithesis.
3
I will be able to keep one clear want driving the whole speech.
🧩 PART 1 · THE SHAPE OF A SOLILOQUY

Look back at what you learned. Hamlet's first soliloquy opens in grief, turns when he names his mother's marriage, and closes by shutting his own mouth. "To be, or not to be" opens with a question, turns on "perchance to dream," and closes on conscience. The pattern is opening, turn, close. Your scene needs the same spine. Use the skeleton below as a container; fill each part with your character's want.

A SCENE SKELETON TO FILL (roughly 20–40 lines)
Opening (the feeling or question)
Where is your character, and what pulls the words out of them right now? Start in the middle of the feeling, not with throat-clearing.
The turn (a new thought or fact)
Something shifts: a memory, a realization, a decision forming. This is where your antithesis often earns its place, holding two possibilities against each other.
Close (the landing)
The character arrives somewhere they were not at the start: a choice, a resolve, a fuller grief. End on a line that could be spoken and remembered.

✏️ PART 2 · DRAFTING TACTICS

Get the thought down first, then shape it to the beat; do not try to write perfect pentameter on the first pass. Say each line aloud as you go, since your ear will catch a limp line faster than your eye. Keep one want in view: if a line does not serve what your character is after, cut it. And do not narrate stage directions in the verse; let the words imply the action, the way Shakespeare's do.

📓
READING JOURNAL
Before you draft in your document, sketch your scene's spine in your Journal: one line naming the opening feeling or question, one line naming the turn, one line naming the close. Then note the single want that runs through all three. Keep this beside you as you draft so the scene does not wander. You will write the full draft in the assignment document, not here.
