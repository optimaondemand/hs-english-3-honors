Lesson 9.01 · Project Craft
The Revision Workshop
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Writing is rewriting. A draft is raw material; revision is where the scene becomes good.
You have a full draft and, likely, a note or two from your teacher. Today you revise with purpose. Revision is not proofreading; it is re-seeing. You will strengthen the shape, sharpen the verse line by line, protect the consistency you built, and only then fix the small errors. Then you submit the graded final.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to revise for shape and sense first, then for line-level craft, then for errors.
2
I will be able to act on feedback deliberately, deciding what to take and why.
🔄 PART 1 · THREE PASSES, IN ORDER

Do not revise everything at once. Make three separate passes, big to small, so you do not polish a line you later cut.

REVISE IN THREE PASSES
Pass 1 · Shape & sense
Does the opening / turn / close still hold? Is the one want clear throughout? Cut or move anything that wanders. This is the pass that matters most.
Pass 2 · The line
Read aloud. Fix limp rhythm, replace a weak verb, make sure your antithesis actually balances. Confirm nothing broke the register or the plot.
Pass 3 · Proofread
Only now: spelling, punctuation (use the module's grammar, no em dashes), and, if you quote or reference the play, a correct MLA citation from Lesson 6.04.

💬 PART 2 · USING FEEDBACK WELL

Feedback is information, not orders. For each comment your teacher left, decide: do you agree, and what will you change? If you disagree, you can keep your choice, but only if you can say why it serves the scene. The one thing you may not do is ignore a note about a plot contradiction; that has to be fixed. Revision is where you show you can hold your own judgment and take good counsel at the same time.

🧩 YOUR TEACHER LEFT THIS NOTE. NOW WHAT?
"This line contradicts what we see happen earlier in the play." What should you do?
📓
READING JOURNAL
Before you revise, log your plan in your Journal: list each piece of feedback you received (or, if none yet, the two weakest spots you already see), and next to each write what you will do about it and why. Then make your three passes in order. Keeping this short revision plan is the habit that turns a draft into a finished piece.
🤖 WORKING WITH CLAUDE ON THIS PROJECT

An AI tool can proofread for spelling and mechanics, a fair use in Pass 3, but do not let it rewrite your lines or "improve" your verse. Use the same narrow rhythm-check step from the Elsinore Stage Workshop and its AI-use log; if it suggests a rewrite, treat it as a question, then fix the line in your own words.

➡️ NOW FINISH IT
🎭
The Elsinore Stage: Final
Submit your revised scene as the graded project. Optionally, record a staged reading for the class gallery.
💪 CARRY THIS FORWARD

The discipline you used here, revise big to small, weigh feedback, protect what you meant, is the same one you will bring to every essay this year. A finished piece is a drafted piece that was taken seriously twice.
