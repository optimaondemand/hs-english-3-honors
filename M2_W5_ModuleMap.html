WEEK 5 MAP
The Self Denied
Publius designed a government for a free people. Emerson demanded that each free person govern an inner life. This week you meet the writer who tests both of them against the hardest fact in American history. In 1845 Frederick Douglass, seven years out of slavery and still legally a fugitive, published the story of his own life: Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass, an American Slave, Written by Himself. As you read the opening chapters, ask what a child needs in order to know who he is, and watch for what happens when each of those things is deliberately withheld. You will read the book's opening chapters as testimony, and read the book itself as an argument aimed at a nation that called itself free. Here is everything for Week 5, in order.
🎯 THE QUESTION BEHIND MODULE 2
How do free people order their common life, how does a free person order an inner one, and what happens when a nation denies that self-government to some of its people?
The third clause of that question has been waiting since Week 1. This week it arrives.
