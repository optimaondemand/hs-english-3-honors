📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A murder is performed in front of a murderer, and the whole court watches the play while two men watch the king.
This is the hour the tragedy turns. Hamlet's staged play reenacts his father's murder; Claudius rises and flees the room; Hamlet finally has what conscience demanded, or believes he does. Today you finish Act III, scene 2 and study the play-within-a-play as a test of truth, then weigh exactly what the test proved. That question is this week's discussion, so read like a lawyer collecting evidence.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to trace the sequence of the Mousetrap from the players' entrance to the king's exit.
2
I will be able to explain how a play-within-a-play works as a device, and what this one is designed to test.
3
I will be able to argue, with lines from 3.2, what Claudius's reaction does and does not prove.
📚 PART 1 · SETTING THE TRAP

Hamlet has arranged for the players to perform a story close enough to his father's death that a guilty man cannot sit through it without some reaction showing. He gives the inner play its name himself, calling it "The Mousetrap. Marry, how? Tropically" (3.2), that is, figuratively, as a trope: a trap catches its target by offering something the target recognizes. As you read to the end of the scene, watch three things closely: how the staged duchess's vow lands on Gertrude sitting nearby, who Hamlet casts as the poisoner and why that casting might unsettle more than one person in the room, and exactly what Claudius does once the poisoning is performed.

🔗 Read: Hamlet, Act III, scene 2, from the court's entrance to the end of the scene. Folger Shakespeare, Hamlet 3.2.
🎪 PART 2 · THE DEVICE: A PLAY INSIDE THE PLAY

A play-within-a-play puts a second, smaller performance inside the drama, and turns the characters into an audience. The device does two things at once. Inside the story, it is Hamlet's experiment: yesterday's mirror theory made operational, with Claudius's face as the readout. Outside the story, it quietly rearranges you. While the court watches the Mousetrap, you are watching the watchers, exactly as Hamlet and Horatio are. For one scene, you and Hamlet share the same job.

HOW A PLAY-WITHIN-A-PLAY WORKS AS A TRAP
THE OUTER PLAY
Hamlet's own world: a court holding a real, unproven crime.
THE INNER PLAY
A staged story close enough to that crime that a guilty man cannot watch it and hold entirely still.
A trap like this works because the target recognizes just enough of the truth to react without meaning to. The reaction, not a confession, is the evidence it produces.
🧩 NOW YOU: PUT THE MOUSETRAP IN ORDER
Click these five moments from Act III, scene 2 in the order they happen on stage.

Why call this the turning point? Because the play's central uncertainty dies here. Hamlet's delay rested on an honest doubt, is the Ghost true, and the Mousetrap removes it for him. From now on the question is not what Hamlet knows but what he will do, and Claudius, who now knows that Hamlet knows, starts acting first. Watch the closing notes of the scene: courtiers manage him like an instrument, and Hamlet snaps that "you cannot play upon me" (3.2), then mutters that "They fool me to the top of my bent" (3.2). The scene ends on a changed man, in a dangerous key: "'Tis now the very witching time of night" (3.2), he says, and, heading to his mother, "I will speak daggers to her, but use none" (3.2).

🤖 WORKING WITH AI

Ask an AI tool whether the Mousetrap proves Claudius's guilt and you will get a fluent answer, likely with quotations. Check every quotation before you trust it; AI tools sometimes attribute lines to the wrong speaker or the wrong scene, and this scene, with a play inside a play, invites exactly that confusion. The Folger text linked above is the arbiter, not the AI's memory or yours.
