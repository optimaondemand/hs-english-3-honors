📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A guilty king kneels to pray. Behind him, sword drawn, stands the man the Mousetrap just convinced. Nothing happens.
Last week Hamlet said he would take "the ghost's word for a thousand pound." Act III, scene 3 hands him the perfect chance to act on it, and he does not take it. Today you read the scene where the two men come closest to each other in the whole play, each alone with his conscience: Claudius trying and failing to pray, and Hamlet talking himself out of the kill. You will also see the sharpest use yet of a device you already know, dramatic irony, because Hamlet's reason for sparing the king rests on a fact that only you know is false.

📚 PART 1 · A WARM-UP BEFORE WE BEGIN
💭 QUICK CHECK-IN (no wrong answer)
Think of a time you wanted to be forgiven for something but were not willing to give up what you gained from it. Which felt more true to you afterward: the wanting, or the not-giving-up?

The villain gets a soliloquy

Until now, the soliloquy has belonged to Hamlet. In scene 3, after sending everyone away, Claudius gets one, and Shakespeare does something risky with it: he makes the murderer honest. Alone, Claudius admits everything the Mousetrap suggested: "O, my offense is rank, it smells to heaven; / It hath the primal eldest curse upon 't, / A brother's murder" (3.3). The "primal eldest curse" reaches back to Cain and Abel, the first murder in Scripture, also a brother killing a brother. Claudius wants to pray and cannot start; he says he stands "like a man to double business bound" (3.3), pulled between repenting and keeping what the murder bought.

Then he asks the question the whole speech turns on: "May one be pardoned and retain th' offense?" (3.3). In the world's "corrupted currents," he admits, a rich criminal can buy his way past justice, "Offense's gilded hand may shove by justice" (3.3), but not in heaven, where every action shows as it truly is and each soul's "audit" must stand as it stands. He knows what real repentance would cost: the crown, the ambition, the queen. He will surrender none of them. Still he tries, ordering his own body into position: "Bow, stubborn knees" (3.3). And there he kneels as Hamlet enters behind him.

⚔️ PART 2 · THE SWORD HELD STILL: REVENGE AND DAMNATION

Hamlet's speech over the kneeling king is not a full meditation like "To be, or not to be." It is a short soliloquy that works like an extended aside: quick, private, decision-making speech, thought moving at the speed of the moment. It begins as action: "Now might I do it pat, now he is a-praying, / And now I'll do 't" (3.3). Then the thinking catches up with the sword. If he kills Claudius at prayer, the king dies with a clean soul and goes to heaven. "A villain kills my father, and for that, / I, his sole son, do this same villain send / To heaven" (3.3). That, he decides, is payment, not payback: "Why, this is hire and salary, not revenge" (3.3).

This is the revenge-and-damnation problem, and it is worth taking seriously on its own terms. In the play's world, when you die matters as much as that you die, because the state of the soul at death decides where it goes. The Ghost complained in Act I of exactly this: killed in his sleep, "full of bread," with no chance to confess, "With all his crimes broad blown, as flush as May" (3.3, as Hamlet recalls it). By that logic, killing Claudius mid-prayer would give the murderer a better death than the victim got. So Hamlet re-sheathes the sword and promises a worse occasion: "When he is drunk asleep, or in his rage, / Or in th' incestuous pleasure of his bed, / At game, a-swearing, or about some act / That has no relish of salvation in 't" (3.3). He exits on a cold line: "This physic but prolongs thy sickly days" (3.3). Whether this logic is conviction or camouflage for one more delay is this week's discussion, so start weighing it now.

THE IDEA BEHIND THIS SCENE: A FALSE PREMISE
A false premise is a belief a decision gets built on that turns out not to be true. The reasoning stacked on top of it can be careful and perfectly logical and still land somewhere wrong, because the floor underneath it was never solid. Dramatic irony is what happens onstage when the audience is handed a piece of the truth that a character does not have. As you read, watch for a decision Hamlet makes on a belief about Claudius's prayer, and watch for a piece of information the scene may give only to you.

READING JOURNAL
Write four or five sentences on Hamlet's decision to wait. To do it well: first restate his reasoning fairly, in your own words, quoting the line that carries it ("Why, this is hire and salary, not revenge," 3.3). Then set the closing couplet beside it and name what you know that Hamlet does not. Finish with the question you will argue in this week's discussion: does his reason read as sincere conviction or as one more excuse to delay? You do not have to decide yet; lay out the evidence on both sides. These notes are your raw material for the discussion post.
🤖 WORKING WITH AI

An AI tool can explain the theology behind this scene, why dying at prayer mattered, quickly and usually accurately. What it cannot do is tell you whether Hamlet believes his own argument, because the text does not say and readers have disagreed for centuries. If you ask, an AI will often pick a side and present it as settled. Treat its answer as one voice in the debate, and hold it to the same standard as any classmate: show me the lines.
