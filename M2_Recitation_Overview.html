THE MODULE 2 RECITATION
Choose a Passage, Learn It by Heart, and Speak It
Read this page in Week 1 so you know what is coming. This module's passages come from Frederick Douglass's Narrative, chapters you will read closely in Weeks 6 and 7 and from its Appendix. The full text is short and public domain, so you may read ahead any time and choose early, or wait and choose once you reach those lessons. Either way, you will record and submit your recitation in Week 7.
🎤 WHAT THIS IS

Once each quarter you memorize and perform a passage from that quarter's text. Last quarter's passage came from Hamlet; this quarter it comes from Frederick Douglass's 1845 Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass, an American Slave. Douglass wrote much of this book to be spoken: it is testimony built for a listening audience, and he went on to become one of the great American orators. Memorizing a passage of it is not busywork. When his words are inside you, you understand his argument differently, and you carry it for life. (Patrick Henry's "Give Me Liberty" speech, which you rehearsed in Week 2, was an ungraded checkpoint on the way here, not this quarter's graded passage.)

🎯 HOW YOU PERFORM IT (READ CAREFULLY)

You recite from memory, with your eyes covered. Wear a blindfold, close your eyes, or turn away from any screen or page. This proves the words are truly memorized and frees you to perform rather than read. There are two ways to do it, and you choose one:

🎥 Option A: Record a video
Record yourself reciting with eyes covered, then submit the video on the Week 7 assignment page. Include a caption or transcript so it can be reviewed without a headset.
💬 Option B: Live with your facilitator
Your facilitator may instead choose to meet with you and hear the recitation live, also with eyes closed. If so, they will schedule it; you do not submit a video.
📜 CHOOSE ONE PASSAGE (15–25 LINES) · TAP TO PREVIEW

Each choice below is a continuous passage of Douglass's own words, about 15 to 25 lines long. Pick the one whose voice you most want to inhabit. Read the whole chapter in context before you decide. Public-domain text: Project Gutenberg, Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass (1845).

The turning point · Narrative, chapter 10
From “This battle with Mr. Covey was the turning-point in my career as a slave” to “must also succeed in killing me.” (about 20 lines)

Douglass's own reflection on the fight that ended his year of being broken by Covey. Choose this if you want a passage built entirely of return: rekindled, revived, recalled, a self coming back. It rewards a voice that can rise from quiet to resolve.

The ships on the bay · Narrative, chapter 10
From “You are loosed from your moorings, and are free” to “Is there any God?” (about 17 lines)

Douglass, alone on the shore of the Chesapeake, speaks directly to the sailing ships that are free to move as he is not. Choose this for raw address and mounting anguish. It rewards short, driving sentences and real breath control.

Freedom awakened · Narrative, chapter 7
From “It had given me a view of my wretched condition, without the remedy” to “breathed in every wind, and moved in every storm.” (about 22 lines)

What it cost Douglass to learn to read: a clear view of his condition with no way out of it, and a restlessness that seemed to live in everything around him. Choose this for its build of parallel clauses (it was heard…it was ever present…I saw…I heard…I felt), a passage that teaches its own rhythm as you say it.

Two Christianities · Narrative, Appendix
From “I love the pure, peaceable, and impartial Christianity of Christ” to “cradle-plunderers for church members.” (about 15 lines)

Douglass's sharpest antithesis: the Christianity he loves set directly against the Christianity he hates, in the same breath. Choose this if you want a passage built for parallelism and pointed contrast, delivered as a formal, public argument rather than a private memory.

You will record your choice in your Reading Journal once you reach these chapters (Weeks 6–7). If you are unsure, read all four aloud once and pick the one your voice returns to.
📊 HOW IT IS GRADED (20 POINTS)
Criteria Strong Developing Pts
Accuracy of text
7 pts		 From memory, eyes covered, faithful to the words; slips are rare and self-recovered. Frequent errors or long stalls, or eyes open / clearly reading. 7
Vocal delivery
7 pts		 Clear and controlled; pace, pause, and stress serve the sense. Rushed or flat; pauses fall mechanically at sentence-ends; hard to follow. 7
Interpretation
6 pts		 Delivery choices show real understanding of the passage's meaning and weight. Recited correctly but with little sense of what the words mean. 6
Total: 20 pts
