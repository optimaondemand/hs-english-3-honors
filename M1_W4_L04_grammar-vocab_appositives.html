Appositives: Adding Without the Dash

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
You often need to slip extra information into a sentence. Commas, used well, let you do it without breaking stride.
When you write about a character, you constantly need to add a quick identification: who they are, what they did, why they matter. The appositive is the tool for that. Today you learn to set one off correctly, and to tell the kind that needs commas from the kind that does not. Two words from this week's scenes round out your Word Study.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to define an appositive and set a nonrestrictive one off with commas.
2
I will be able to tell a nonrestrictive appositive (commas) from a restrictive one (no commas).
3
I will be able to use two words from Act III precisely in sentences of my own.
✏️ PART 1 · THE APPOSITIVE

An appositive is a noun phrase that renames the noun right beside it. In "Claudius, the murderer of his brother, kneels to pray," the phrase "the murderer of his brother" renames Claudius. It adds information without a second sentence and without a dash. The question is only whether to fence it with commas, and that depends on one thing: is the information extra, or is it needed to identify the noun?

If the sentence already makes clear who or what you mean, the appositive is nonrestrictive (extra), and you set it off with commas. If the appositive is what pins down which one you mean, it is restrictive (needed), and you use no commas. A quick test: if you can lift the phrase out and still know exactly who is meant, wrap it in commas.

COMMAS OR NO COMMAS?
NONRESTRICTIVE (extra) · USE COMMAS
Claudius, the murderer of his brother, kneels to pray.
There is only one Claudius; his crime is added information. Lift it out and the sentence still identifies him. Commas.
RESTRICTIVE (needed) · NO COMMAS
The line "hire and salary, not revenge" reveals Hamlet's reasoning.
"The line" could be any line; the quotation is what tells us which one. It is needed, so no commas fence it off.
✏️ SELF-CHECK: COMMAS OR NO COMMAS?
Which sentence punctuates its appositive correctly? ("Ophelia" already tells you exactly who is meant; her identity as Polonius's daughter is extra information about her.)
✏️ SELF-CHECK: RESTRICTIVE OR NOT?
Which sentence punctuates its appositive correctly? ("The word" could mean any word; only the appositive tells you which one.)

📕 PART 2 · WORD STUDY (from Act III)
WordIn the playWhat it meansWord origin
rank
adjective		 Claudius: "O, my offense is rank, it smells to heaven" (3.3). Growing too thickly; foul-smelling; offensive to the point of being obvious. From Old English ranc ("proud, overgrown, full-grown"), a Germanic word that came to mean growing too thickly or smelling strongly offensive.
unction
noun		 Hamlet warns Gertrude, "Lay not that flattering unction to your soul" (3.4). A soothing ointment; by extension, a comforting idea used to smooth over a hard truth. From Latin unctionem, from unguere ("to anoint, to smear with oil"); literally "an anointing," extended figuratively to a soothing balm for the conscience.
📓
READING JOURNAL · WORD STUDY
Add both words to the Word Study section of your Journal. For each, write one new sentence of your own on a subject unrelated to Hamlet, keeping the meaning exact. Make the sentence show the meaning, not just contain the word. For unction, aim for the figurative sense: a comforting idea someone uses to avoid a hard truth. Notice how unction's root sense of "anointing with oil" still echoes in its figurative use as something that smooths over a truth.

💪 CARRY THIS FORWARD

The appositive is how careful writers pack a sentence without cluttering it. Reach for it whenever you introduce a character or a term, and let the commas signal whether the information is extra or essential.
